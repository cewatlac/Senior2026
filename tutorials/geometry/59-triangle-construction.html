Class 05 — Triangle Construction

Given two vertices and the two distances to a third, where does the third vertex go? We build the triangle from a base and a height, recover the two mirror solutions, classify feasibility with the triangle inequality, and handle every degenerate case cleanly — all on the dot/cross/orient substrate from Classes 01–04.

🎯 1 — Problem Definition

Triangle construction asks: from partial data about a triangle, recover its actual vertices. The central version in this class fixes two vertices and the two distances from them to the third:

Given A, B, and distances b = |AC|, a = |BC| — where is C? Do three given side lengths even form a triangle? When the third vertex exists, why are there usually two of them? What happens when the triangle is flat (zero area)?

The natural picture is two circles: C lies on the circle of radius b around A and simultaneously on the circle of radius a around B. Their intersection points are the candidate vertices. We will not solve that with messy circle algebra, though — we decompose the displacement of C into a slide along the base AB plus a perpendicular lift, which turns the whole thing into one square root and a Class 02 perpendicular.

Why this is the spine of the circle classes. triangleThirdPoint is exactly the engine behind circle-circle intersection (Class 06): two intersecting circles define a triangle whose third vertex is the intersection point. Getting the two-solution and degenerate handling right here pays off directly there.

🧠 2 — Intuition from Scratch

2.1 — Two circles meeting

Pin one end of a string of length b at A and another of length a at B. The free end of the first sweeps a circle around A; the free end of the second sweeps a circle around B. The third vertex C must lie where both strings can reach at once — the intersection of the two circles. Two circles in general position meet in two points, one on each side of the line AB; those are the two mirror triangles.

Diagram · Two circles around A and B meet at C above and below the base

2.2 — From circles to a base-and-height decomposition

Intersecting circles directly is algebra-heavy. The cleaner route: walk from A along the base toward B by some signed amount x to reach the foot of the perpendicular under C, then lift straight off the base by the height h. That replaces two circle equations with one right triangle on each side of the foot.

👁 3 — Visual Mental Model

3.1 — Foot on the base, then lift by the height

Drop a perpendicular from C to the base line AB; call the landing point the foot F. The whole position of C is then "slide from A to F along the base, then rise by h perpendicular to it." The base splits into two right triangles sharing the height h: one with legs x, h and hypotenuse b, the other with legs c − x, h and hypotenuse a.

Diagram · The foot F, the slide x, and the height h form two right triangles

3.2 — The two mirror solutions

Once the foot and the height are known, lifting C "up" (left of directed A → B) or "down" (right of it) both give a valid triangle. They are reflections of each other across the base line, so they share the same side lengths and area. We pick one with an explicit side flag.

Diagram · Mirror solutions: C and C' reflected across the base AB

🧮 4 — Formal Abstraction

4.1 — Naming the quantities

Let the base length be c = |A − B|. Let b = |A − C| and a = |B − C| be the two given distances (named so that side a is opposite vertex A, matching the usual convention). Let x be the signed distance from A to the foot F measured along the unit base direction, and h ≥ 0 the height.

4.2 — The two key formulas

The two right triangles give two Pythagorean equations sharing h²:

b^2 = x^2 + h^2                 (triangle A–F–C)
a^2 = (c - x)^2 + h^2           (triangle B–F–C)

Subtracting eliminates h² and solves for the foot offset, then back-substitution gives the height:

x  = (b^2 - a^2 + c^2) / (2c)
h^2 = b^2 - x^2          ⇒   h = sqrt(b^2 - x^2)   (requires b^2 - x^2 >= 0)

4.3 — Feasibility is the sign of the radicand

The construction succeeds exactly when h² = b² − x² ≥ 0. This single condition is algebraically equivalent to the triangle inequality on a, b, c. The clean way to see it is the factored form of sixteen times the squared area:

16 * Area^2 = (a + b + c)(-a + b + c)(a - b + c)(a + b - c)

The product is non-negative exactly when every triangle-inequality factor is non-negative, i.e. when no side exceeds the sum of the other two. So "the height is real" and "the sides satisfy the triangle inequality" are the same statement.

Diagram · Impossible triangle — one side too long, the circles never meet

4.4 — Feasibility classification

Condition on sidesGeometryReturn code
Some side > sum of other two (or a negative length)Circles never meet — impossible.-1
Some side = sum of other twoCircles tangent — flat, zero-area triangle.0
Every side < sum of other twoTwo genuine triangles (mirror pair).+1

💡 5 — Key Observations

  • O1 — Decompose, don't intersect. Writing C = foot + h·normal replaces two circle equations with one slide and one lift — a single square root, no quadratic in two variables.
  • O2 — The foot offset is forced. x = (b² − a² + c²) / (2c) is completely determined by the three lengths; only the side of the lift is free.
  • O3 — Feasibility = real height = triangle inequality. b² − x² ≥ 0 is exactly "the three lengths satisfy the triangle inequality" (§4.3).
  • O4 — Two solutions, mirror images. h > 0 gives two points reflected across AB; they differ only by the sign on the normal, i.e. by orientation.
  • O5 — Height zero ⇒ one solution. When h = 0 the two mirrors collapse onto the base line into a single degenerate (collinear) point.
  • O6 — Side choice is an orientation choice. Lifting along perpCCW(dir) puts C on the left (orient(A,B,C) = +1); negating it puts C on the right. The side flag selects which.
  • O7 — Area two ways. The same area comes from Heron's formula on the sides or from ½ · base · height; equating them gives h = 2·Area / c, a useful cross-check.

⚙ 6 — Algorithm / Method Explanation

6.1 — Constructing the third vertex, step by step

  1. Compute the base length c = |A − B|. If c = 0, the base direction does not exist. If a = b = 0, the unique collapsed answer is C = A = B; otherwise the construction reports found = 0 because the answer is either impossible (a ≠ b) or non-unique (a = b > 0).
  2. Compute the foot offset x = (b² − a² + c²) / (2c).
  3. Compute h² = b² − x². If h² < 0 the distances cannot meet — report failure.
  4. Take h = sqrt(h²). Build the unit base direction dir = (B − A)/c and the foot F = A + dir·x.
  5. If h = 0 the answer is the single point F on the base — report one degenerate solution.
  6. Otherwise lift: C = F + perpCCW(dir)·(h·side), where side = +1 (left) or −1 (right). The mirror is obtained by flipping side.

6.2 — How the function reports its outcome

A single found out-parameter encodes the three possible outcomes, so the caller always knows what the returned point means:

foundMeaningReturned point
0No unique usable point: negative length, invalid side flag, infeasible distances, impossible coincident-base case, or non-unique coincident-base circle-locus case.Unused placeholder; caller must not read it as a constructed vertex.
1Degenerate single solution: either a flat point on the base line (h = 0) or the fully collapsed case A = B = C.The single constructed point.
2Two solutions exist; this is the one on the requested side.The off-line vertex; flip side for the other.

6.3 — Base direction and the perpendicular lift

The lift direction is the Class 02 perpendicular of the unit base direction. perpCCW(dir) = (−dir.y, dir.x) is the left normal; using h·side as its length signs the lift. Because dir is already a unit vector and perpCCW preserves length, the offset has magnitude exactly h.

Diagram · Projection foot on AB plus the perpendicular offset by h

6.4 — The degenerate (collinear, zero-area) case

When the height is exactly zero, there is nothing to lift: the two mirror candidates both sit on the base line and coincide at the foot. The "triangle" is flat — its three vertices are collinear and its area is zero. The function reports this with found = 1 and returns that single point, rather than pretending two distinct vertices exist.

Diagram · Degenerate triangle — h = 0, C collapses onto the base line

🔒 7 — Invariants

State invariants

  • I1 — found is exhaustive and exclusive. Every call sets found to exactly one of {0, 1, 2}; the returned point is meaningful only when found ≥ 1.
  • I2 — Distances are honoured when found ≥ 1. The returned C satisfies |A − C| = b and |B − C| = a up to floating tolerance, by construction from the two Pythagorean equations.
  • I3 — Side flag controls orientation. When found = 2, orient(A, B, C) = side: +1 lifts left/CCW, −1 lifts right/CW.

Transition invariants

  • I4 — Mirror symmetry. Calling with +side and −side returns two points that are reflections of each other across the line AB; their foot and height are identical.
  • I5 — Feasibility agrees with the classifier under valid construction preconditions. Given non-negative lengths, a valid side flag, and a non-degenerate base, triangleThirdPoint reports found = 0 exactly when triangleFeasible(c, b, a) = −1, and found = 1 exactly when it returns 0.

Termination invariants

The construction is straight-line arithmetic — one square root, a handful of multiplies, no loops or recursion — so it returns in O(1).

Failure mode — preconditions. The distances a, b must be non-negative (they are lengths); a negative input reports found = 0. The base must be non-degenerate for a direction to exist. If A = B, the function only returns a unique point in the fully collapsed case a = b = 0; otherwise it reports found = 0 because the construction has no unique vertex to return.

📜 8 — Proof of Correctness

8.1 — Deriving the foot offset x

Place the base on a local axis with A at coordinate 0 and B at coordinate c, and let the foot sit at coordinate x with the apex at height h. The two right triangles give b² = x² + h² and a² = (c − x)² + h². Subtract the second from the first:

b^2 - a^2 = x^2 - (c - x)^2
          = x^2 - (c^2 - 2cx + x^2)
          = 2cx - c^2
⇒  x = (b^2 - a^2 + c^2) / (2c)

The division is safe because c > 0 whenever A ≠ B.

Diagram · Local frame for the derivation — A at 0, B at c, apex (x, h)

8.2 — Recovering the height h

From b² = x² + h² we get h² = b² − x², hence h = √(b² − x²), taking the non-negative root (height is a length). The apex in the local frame is (x, h); mapping back to world coordinates is "start at A, move x along the unit base direction, then h along its perpendicular," which is exactly A + dir·x + perpCCW(dir)·h.

8.3 — Feasibility equals the triangle inequality

The construction needs h² = b² − x² ≥ 0. Substituting x = (b² − a² + c²)/(2c) and simplifying yields the symmetric identity

16 * Area^2 = (a + b + c)(-a + b + c)(a - b + c)(a + b - c)
            = 4 c^2 (b^2 - x^2)

Since 4c² > 0, the sign of b² − x² matches the sign of the four-factor product. Every factor is non-negative precisely when each side is at most the sum of the other two — the triangle inequality. Thus h² ≥ 0 ⟺ the lengths form a triangle, with equality (h = 0) exactly at the degenerate, collinear case where one factor vanishes.

8.4 — Heron and the area / height relationship

Taking the square root of the identity above and dividing by 4 gives Heron's formula with semi-perimeter s = (a + b + c)/2:

Area = sqrt( s (s - a)(s - b)(s - c) )

Independently the triangle's area is ½ · base · height = ½ · c · h. Equating the two expressions gives the cross-check h = 2·Area / c, confirming the height we computed from the Pythagorean route agrees with the Heron area.

8.5 — Why the side flag yields the correct orientation

The lift uses perpCCW(dir), the left normal of the directed base A → B. For a point displaced from the base by a positive multiple of the left normal, cross(B − A, C − A) > 0, so orient(A, B, C) = +1. Multiplying the lift by side = −1 flips the sign of the cross product, giving orient = −1. Hence the returned point lies on exactly the requested side, and the two choices are mirror images across AB.

⏱ 9 — Complexity Analysis

OperationTimeNotes
triangleFeasibleO(1)A few comparisons via cmp.
triangleThirdPoint (preconditioned, single side)O(1)One dist, one safeSqrt, a constant number of multiplies and adds.
triangle3rdPoint (recommended, returns both mirrors)O(1)Same arithmetic; returns vector<Point> of size 0 (infeasible), 1 (tangent), or 2 (mirror pair, +1 side first).
triangleAreaHeronO(1)One safeSqrt over the semi-perimeter product.
Recovering both verticesO(1)Call once per side, or lift with ±h.

Everything here is constant-time. When triangle construction sits inside a loop — for instance computing every circle-circle intersection across n circles — the asymptotic cost belongs to the loop, not to this primitive.

📐 10 — Core Template Philosophy

  • Report both, fail clean. A construction that can yield two points must expose both, and one that can fail must say so explicitly. The found ∈ {0,1,2} contract makes the caller handle every outcome.
  • Decompose into base + height. Prefer the slide-and-lift decomposition over raw circle intersection: one square root, no two-variable quadratic, and it reuses the Class 02 perpendicular directly.
  • Feasibility through the radicand. Let h² ≥ 0 be the single gate; it is the triangle inequality in disguise, so there is no separate inequality bookkeeping to keep in sync.
  • Side as orientation. Encode the left/right choice as a ±1 flag tied to orient, so the geometry and the sign convention never drift apart.
  • Const-reference inputs, documented preconditions. Points pass by const Point&; non-negativity of lengths and A ≠ B are stated where they matter.

💻 11 — Master / Base Implementation

Standalone, compiles with g++ -std=c++17. The first block repeats just enough of the Class 01–04 substrate for the file to build on its own; the Class 05 additions follow the divider.

#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;

using T     = long double;
using Point = complex<T>;

#define X real()
#define Y imag()

const long double EPS = 1e-12L;
const T PI = acosl(-1.0L);

// ---- rotation helper (typed function, not a macro) ----------------
Point rot(const Point& v, T t) { return v * polar((T)1, t); }

// ---- Class 01-04 substrate ----------------------------------------
template<class U> int cmpInt(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) { return (a > b) - (a < b); }
template<class U> int cmpFloat(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    long double d = (long double)a - (long double)b;
    return (d > EPS) - (d < -EPS);
}
template<class U> int cmp(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    if constexpr (is_floating_point_v<U>) return cmpFloat(a, b);
    else return cmpInt(a, b);
}
T safeSqrt(T x) { if (cmp(x) == 0) return 0; return (T)sqrtl((long double)x); }
T dot  (const Point& a, const Point& b) { return real(conj(a) * b); }
T cross(const Point& a, const Point& b) { return imag(conj(a) * b); }
T len (const Point& a) { return (T)hypotl((long double)a.X, (long double)a.Y); }
T dist(const Point& a, const Point& b) { return len(a - b); }
bool samePoint(const Point& a, const Point& b) {
    return cmp(a.X, b.X) == 0 && cmp(a.Y, b.Y) == 0;
}
Point perpCCW(const Point& v) { return Point(-v.Y, v.X); }   // left normal
int orient(const Point& a, const Point& b, const Point& p) {
    return cmp(cross(b - a, p - a));                          // +1 left, -1 right, 0 collinear
}

// ===================================================================
// CLASS 05 — triangle construction
// ===================================================================

// Feasibility from three side lengths x, y, z.
// Returns: -1 impossible, 0 degenerate (one side == sum of the other two),
//          +1 strict triangle.
// Precondition: lengths are intended non-negative; a negative input => impossible.
int triangleFeasible(T x, T y, T z) {
    if (cmp(x) < 0 || cmp(y) < 0 || cmp(z) < 0) return -1;  // negative length
    if (cmp(x + y, z) < 0) return -1;
    if (cmp(y + z, x) < 0) return -1;
    if (cmp(x + z, y) < 0) return -1;
    if (cmp(x + y, z) == 0) return 0;
    if (cmp(y + z, x) == 0) return 0;
    if (cmp(x + z, y) == 0) return 0;
    return 1;
}

// Locate the third vertex C given base endpoints A, B and the two distances
// b = |A - C| and a = |B - C|.
//   side = +1 : C on the LEFT of directed A->B  (orient(A,B,C) = +1, CCW)
//   side = -1 : C on the RIGHT of directed A->B (orient(A,B,C) = -1, CW)
// found (out):
//   0 -> no unique usable point: negative length, infeasible distances,
//        or A == B with infinitely many possible vertices (a == b > 0) / a != b
//   1 -> degenerate single point: a flat point on the base (height == 0),
//        or the fully collapsed case A == B == C (a == b == 0)
//   2 -> a genuine off-line vertex on the requested side; mirror is the other side
// Preconditions: a >= 0, b >= 0, and side is exactly +1 or -1.
// Invalid lengths or invalid side flags report found = 0.
Point triangleThirdPoint(const Point& A, const Point& B,
                         T b, T a, int side, int& found) {
    if (cmp(a) < 0 || cmp(b) < 0) {    // negative distance is not a valid length
        found = 0;
        return A;                     // value unused when found == 0
    }
    if (side != +1 && side != -1) {   // invalid side flag
        found = 0;
        return A;
    }
    T c = dist(A, B);                 // base length |A - B|
    if (cmp(c) == 0) {                // A and B coincide -> no base direction
        if (cmp(a) == 0 && cmp(b) == 0) {
            found = 1;                // all three points collapse to one: C = A = B
            return A;
        }
        found = 0;                    // a != b impossible, or a == b > 0 non-unique (whole circle)
        return A;                     // value unused when found == 0
    }
    // foot offset along the base:  x = (b^2 - a^2 + c^2) / (2c)
    T x  = (b*b - a*a + c*c) / (2*c);
    T h2 = b*b - x*x;                 // squared height
    if (cmp(h2) < 0) {                // circles do not meet -> infeasible
        found = 0;
        return A;
    }
    T h = safeSqrt(h2);
    Point dir  = (B - A) / c;         // unit base direction
    Point foot = A + dir * x;         // foot of the perpendicular on the base
    if (cmp(h) == 0) {                // circles tangent -> single point on the base
        found = 1;
        return foot;
    }
    Point nrm = perpCCW(dir);         // unit left normal (the +1 side)
    found = 2;
    return foot + nrm * (h * (T)side);
}

// ---- Recommended vector-return API ---------------------------------
// triangle3rdPoint(A, B, b, a): same construction, but returns BOTH
// mirror solutions at once. Empty vector = infeasible; size 1 = the
// tangent/collapsed case; size 2 = the genuine mirror pair, with the
// "+1 side" (CCW from base A->B) listed first.
//
// Prefer this in new code: callers no longer have to track a `side` flag
// or an integer `found` count, and the same call covers every outcome.
vector<Point> triangle3rdPoint(const Point& A, const Point& B, T b, T a) {
    if (cmp(a) < 0 || cmp(b) < 0) return {};      // negative length: infeasible
    T c = dist(A, B);
    if (cmp(c) == 0) {
        if (cmp(a) == 0 && cmp(b) == 0) return { A };   // all three points coincide
        return {};                                  // no base direction, no unique answer
    }
    T x  = (b*b - a*a + c*c) / (2*c);
    T h2 = b*b - x*x;
    if (cmp(h2) < 0) return {};                    // circles do not meet
    T h = safeSqrt(h2);
    Point dir  = (B - A) / c;
    Point foot = A + dir * x;
    if (cmp(h) == 0) return { foot };              // tangent: a single point on the base
    Point nrm  = perpCCW(dir);
    return { foot + nrm * h, foot - nrm * h };     // +1 side first, then mirror
}

// Area of a triangle with sides a, b, c via Heron's formula.
// Precondition: a, b, c form a valid (possibly degenerate) triangle.
T triangleAreaHeron(T a, T b, T c) {
    T s = (a + b + c) / 2;
    return safeSqrt(s * (s - a) * (s - b) * (s - c));
}
Two macros that look identical to fields but are not. #define X real() makes v.X expand to v.real(); #define Y imag() likewise. rot is a typed helper function, never a macro — and never named rotate (which collides with std::rotate).

⚠ 14 — Common Pitfalls / Bugs

  • P1 — Returning only one solution. A generic third-vertex query has two answers. Silently returning the "up" one hides the mirror; expose the side flag (or return both) so the caller chooses.
  • P2 — Swapping which distance is a and which is b. Here b = |A − C| and a = |B − C|. Swapping them mirrors the foot offset to c − x and moves C. Keep the "side a is opposite vertex A" convention.
  • P3 — Taking sqrt of a negative radicand. When the triangle is infeasible, h² < 0. Test the sign first; only then call safeSqrt. A raw sqrt on a negative gives NaN that silently poisons the result.
  • P4 — Forgetting the degenerate (h = 0) case. If you always lift by ±h you will "find" two coincident points and may report a phantom second triangle. Detect h = 0 and report a single solution.
  • P5 — Dividing by c without checking A ≠ B. A coincident base gives c = 0 and a division by zero. Guard first.
  • P6 — Using raw < / == on the feasibility tests. Float sums need cmp with EPS, or a true-degenerate triangle reads as impossible (or a true-impossible reads as degenerate) right on the boundary.
  • P7 — Not signing the lift. Lifting by h·perpCCW(dir) always lands on the left. Multiply by side to honour a requested right-hand placement; otherwise orientation-sensitive callers get the wrong winding.

Reflex rules (contest speed)

Foot offset: x = (b² − a² + c²) / (2c). Height: h = √(b² − x²). Lift: C = foot + perpCCW(dir)·(h·side). Feasible ⟺ h² ≥ 0 ⟺ triangle inequality. h = 0 ⇒ one collinear solution. Never sqrt before checking the radicand's sign.

Common confusion

"My triangle came out on the wrong side." The lift defaults to the left normal perpCCW(dir). If you need the clockwise placement, pass side = −1; do not negate the base direction, which would also move the foot.

🌿 16 — Variants & Extensions

16.1 — Circle-circle intersection (the same engine)

Two circles with centres A, B and radii b, a intersect exactly where a triangle with base AB and those two side lengths has its third vertex. What changes: nothing in the math — the centres are A, B, the radii are the distances. Why: it reuses the third-point engine verbatim. When to use: Class 06 / 60 builds full circle-circle intersection on top of this.

Recommended (vector-return). The cleanest CP-style wrapper is a single line on top of the vector API: every possible outcome (0, 1, or 2 points) flows out as a vector<Point>.

// Both intersection points of circles (A, rA) and (B, rB), if any.
// Returns 0, 1, or 2 points; the "+1 side" (CCW from base) comes first.
vector<Point> circleCircleIntersection(const Point& A, T rA,
                                       const Point& B, T rB) {
    return triangle3rdPoint(A, B, rA, rB);
}

Defensive count/output-parameter variant. The older two-output form is still useful when you are programming defensively (or porting code that expects the found flag).

// found:
//   0 -> no finite unique output from this wrapper
//        (none, invalid radius, or coincident circles with infinitely many intersections)
//   1 -> tangent, single point
//   2 -> two intersection points
void circleCircle(const Point& A, T rA, const Point& B, T rB,
                  Point& P1, Point& P2, int& found) {
    P1 = triangleThirdPoint(A, B, rA, rB, +1, found);   // rA = |A-C|, rB = |B-C|
    if (found == 2) P2 = triangleThirdPoint(A, B, rA, rB, -1, found);
    // found is 0 when this wrapper has no finite unique output,
    // 1 for tangent, or 2 for two finite intersection points.
}

16.2 — Area straight from the base and apex

Once C is constructed, the signed area is immediate from the cross product, no Heron needed: Area = ½ · cross(B − A, C − A) (positive when C is on the left). The magnitude matches triangleAreaHeron; the sign carries the orientation.

T triangleSignedArea(const Point& A, const Point& B, const Point& C) {
    return cross(B - A, C - A) / 2;   // sign = orientation, |value| = area
}

16.3 — Integer side-length feasibility without floating error

For problems where the three side lengths are integers, decide feasibility exactly by sorting the lengths and comparing the sum of the two smaller sides against the largest side. This avoids sqrt and avoids EPS entirely. When to use: direct integer side-length inputs, not squared distances.

// Exact feasibility from integer side lengths.
// Returns -1 impossible, 0 degenerate, +1 strict triangle.
int triangleFeasibleInt(long long x, long long y, long long z) {
    if (x < 0 || y < 0 || z < 0) return -1;
    // sort so z is the largest; then the only inequality that can fail is x + y vs z
    long long s[3] = {x, y, z};
    sort(s, s + 3);
    if (s[0] + s[1] < s[2]) return -1;   // strict failure
    if (s[0] + s[1] == s[2]) return 0;   // exact equality -> degenerate
    return 1;
}
Why integers can be exact. With integer side lengths, x + y and z are integers, so < and == are exact — no EPS needed. If your input is squared distances, do not use this side-length template directly; compare with a squared-distance-specific method instead.

🚀 17 — Problem-Based Applications & Training Drills

Training drill — locate the third vertex of a triangle the canonical construction pattern, not a contest problem

Scope. This is a training drill for the base-and-height construction, not a full contest problem. It isolates the third-vertex computation before Class 06 uses the same engine for circle-circle intersection.

Story. Given two vertices A and B and the two distances b = |AC| and a = |BC|, output the left-hand third vertex C, report that the unique construction is degenerate, or report that there is no unique usable point.

Abstraction. This is exactly triangleThirdPoint(A, B, b, a, +1, found), branching on found ∈ {0, 1, 2}.

Why this geometry model fits. The displacement of C decomposes into a slide along the base by x = (b² − a² + c²)/(2c) and a perpendicular lift by h = √(b² − x²); the sign of h² decides feasibility (§8.3).

Algorithm. Read A, B, b, a; construct with side = +1; print according to found, where found = 0 means no unique usable point.

#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;

using T     = long double;
using Point = complex<T>;
#define X real()
#define Y imag()

const long double EPS = 1e-12L;

template<class U> int cmpFloat(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    long double d = (long double)a - (long double)b;
    return (d > EPS) - (d < -EPS);
}
template<class U> int cmp(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    if constexpr (is_floating_point_v<U>) return cmpFloat(a, b);
    else return (a > b) - (a < b);
}
T safeSqrt(T x) { if (cmp(x) == 0) return 0; return (T)sqrtl((long double)x); }
T len (const Point& a) { return (T)hypotl((long double)a.X, (long double)a.Y); }
T dist(const Point& a, const Point& b) { return len(a - b); }
Point perpCCW(const Point& v) { return Point(-v.Y, v.X); }

Point triangleThirdPoint(const Point& A, const Point& B,
                         T b, T a, int side, int& found) {
    if (cmp(a) < 0 || cmp(b) < 0) { found = 0; return A; }  // negative length
    if (side != +1 && side != -1) {                          // invalid side flag
        found = 0;
        return A;
    }
    T c = dist(A, B);
    if (cmp(c) == 0) {                                       // coincident base
        if (cmp(a) == 0 && cmp(b) == 0) { found = 1; return A; }  // C = A = B
        found = 0; return A;                                // non-unique or impossible
    }
    T x  = (b*b - a*a + c*c) / (2*c);
    T h2 = b*b - x*x;
    if (cmp(h2) < 0) { found = 0; return A; }
    T h = safeSqrt(h2);
    Point dir  = (B - A) / c;
    Point foot = A + dir * x;
    if (cmp(h) == 0) { found = 1; return foot; }
    found = 2;
    return foot + perpCCW(dir) * (h * (T)side);
}

int main() {
    ios::sync_with_stdio(false);
    cin.tie(nullptr);

    long double ax, ay, bx, by, b, a;
    while (cin >> ax >> ay >> bx >> by >> b >> a) {
        int found;
        Point C = triangleThirdPoint(Point(ax, ay), Point(bx, by),
                                     b, a, +1, found);
        if (found == 0) {
            cout << "NO_UNIQUE_POINT\n";
        } else if (found == 1) {
            cout << fixed << setprecision(6)
                 << "DEGENERATE " << C.X << ' ' << C.Y << '\n';
        } else {
            cout << fixed << setprecision(6)
                 << C.X << ' ' << C.Y << '\n';
        }
    }
    return 0;
}

Complexity. O(1) per query.

Output meaning. NO_UNIQUE_POINT covers impossible distances and also non-unique cases such as A = B, a = b > 0, where infinitely many valid points exist.

Pitfalls. Keep b = |AC| and a = |BC| in that order. The only coincident-base case with a unique answer is A = B and a = b = 0; otherwise the base direction does not define a unique construction. As a worked check: A = (0,0), B = (4,0), b = 3, a = 5 gives the left vertex (0, 3); flipping the side gives the mirror (0, −3).

Problem URL. not an OJ problem; training drill only.

Training drill — feasibility from three side lengths triangle-inequality classifier, not a contest problem

Scope. A pattern drill for the feasibility classifier, not a standalone contest problem. It shows the impossible / degenerate / strict trichotomy that gates the construction above.

Story. Given three side lengths, decide whether they form a strict triangle, a flat (degenerate) one, or no triangle at all.

Abstraction. This is triangleFeasible(x, y, z): reject negatives and strict inequality failures (−1), flag exact equalities as degenerate (0), otherwise accept (+1).

Why this geometry model fits. Feasibility is the triangle inequality, which §8.3 shows is exactly the condition h² ≥ 0 for a real apex height. Equality is the tangent (zero-area) case.

Algorithm. For integer inputs, sort the three lengths and compare the two smallest against the largest — exact, no sqrt, no EPS.

#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;

// Exact feasibility from integer side lengths.
// -1 impossible, 0 degenerate (flat), +1 strict triangle.
int triangleFeasibleInt(long long x, long long y, long long z) {
    if (x < 0 || y < 0 || z < 0) return -1;
    long long s[3] = {x, y, z};
    sort(s, s + 3);                      // s[2] is the longest side
    if (s[0] + s[1] < s[2])  return -1;   // longest exceeds the other two
    if (s[0] + s[1] == s[2]) return 0;    // exactly flat
    return 1;                             // genuine triangle
}

int main() {
    ios::sync_with_stdio(false);
    cin.tie(nullptr);

    long long x, y, z;
    while (cin >> x >> y >> z) {
        int r = triangleFeasibleInt(x, y, z);
        if      (r < 0) cout << "IMPOSSIBLE\n";
        else if (r == 0) cout << "DEGENERATE\n";
        else            cout << "TRIANGLE\n";
    }
    return 0;
}

Complexity. O(1) per query (sorting three elements is constant).

Pitfalls. Use the sorted comparison so only one inequality can fail; check the strict failure before the equality. For real-valued lengths switch to the floating triangleFeasible with cmp, since the boundary needs EPS.

📝 18 — Final Summary

The mental model

  • Two circles around A and B meet at the third vertex.
  • Decompose: slide to the foot by x, lift by h.
  • Foot: x = (b² − a² + c²)/(2c).
  • Height: h = √(b² − x²).
  • Two solutions: lift left or right (mirror across AB).
  • Area: Heron, or ½·base·h.

The hard rules

  • Feasible ⟺ h² ≥ 0 ⟺ triangle inequality.
  • found: 0 no unique usable point, 1 one point, 2 two mirror choices.
  • Never sqrt a negative radicand — test sign first.
  • Guard A ≠ B before dividing by c.
  • Sign the lift with side for the right orientation.

Class 05 turns "two distances from two known points" into an explicit vertex, with honest reporting of zero, one, or two solutions. The same base-and-height engine drives circle-circle intersection in Class 06; the feasibility radicand and Heron identity reappear in the circle-cover and area work of Class 07 and Pick's-theorem area in Class 08.

What's next. Class 06 takes this construction directly into circles: intersecting two circles, intersecting a circle with a line, and finding tangents — all resting on the third-vertex engine built here.

📮 19 — Footer / Contact

Runtime Validation Checklist

Before using triangle construction in a contest solution, verify each item below. The Class 01 numeric checklist and the Class 02 orientation checklist still apply underneath.

Feasibility & reporting

  • Side lengths screened for negativity and the triangle inequality before constructing.
  • The found out-parameter is read on every call; the returned point is used only when found ≥ 1.
  • The degenerate case (found = 1, h = 0) is handled as a single point, not a phantom pair.
  • The infeasible case (found = 0) never has its placeholder point treated as a vertex.

Construction & orientation

  • Base direction divides by c = |A − B| only after handling the coincident-base case A = B.
  • The radicand b² − x² has its sign checked with cmp before safeSqrt.
  • The lift is signed by side, and orient(A, B, C) = side is confirmed when two solutions exist.
  • The mirror solution is obtained by flipping side (or lifting by −h).
  • side is exactly +1 or −1; invalid side flags are rejected with found = 0.

Spot checks (run mentally before submitting)

  • triangleFeasible(3,4,5) = +1; (1,1,2) = 0; (1,1,5) = −1.
  • triangleThirdPoint((0,0),(4,0), 3, 5, +1) = (0, 3), found = 2; the −1 side gives (0, −3).
  • Equilateral side 2: triangleThirdPoint((0,0),(2,0), 2, 2, +1) = (1, √3) ≈ (1, 1.7321).
  • Degenerate base 4, b = 1, a = 3: returns (1, 0), found = 1.
  • Impossible b = a = 1 on base 4: found = 0.
  • triangleThirdPoint((0,0),(0,0), 0, 0, +1) returns (0,0), found = 1.
  • triangleThirdPoint((0,0),(4,0), 3, 5, 0) returns found = 0 because side = 0 is invalid.
  • triangleThirdPoint((0,0),(4,0), 3, 5, 2) returns found = 0 because side = 2 is invalid.
  • triangleAreaHeron(3,4,5) = 6, matching ½·4·3 = 6.
