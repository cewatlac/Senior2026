Class 06 — Circle Intersection & Tangents

Where do two circles meet — never, once, twice, or everywhere? We classify the five configurations by the distance between centers, recover the intersection points by reusing the Class 05 third-vertex engine, intersect a circle with a line, and construct tangent points and tangent lengths from an external point.

🎯 1 — Problem Definition

A circle is a center and a radius. Two circles, or a circle and a line, raise the natural intersection questions — and circles add a wrinkle that lines never have: two identical positive-radius circles meet at infinitely many points, while two identical zero-radius circles are the same single point and therefore share exactly one point. So the answer count is not just 0, 1, or 2.

Where do circles (A, r₁) and (B, r₂) intersect — 0, 1, 2, or ∞ points? Where does a line cross a circle? From an external point, how long is the tangent, and where does it touch? How do I tell apart "no solution" from "infinitely many"?

The unifying idea: a point on both circles is at distance r₁ from A and r₂ from B — exactly the third vertex of a triangle with base A–B and side lengths r₁, r₂. So circle-circle intersection is the Class 05 construction, with the circle centers playing the role of the base endpoints and the radii playing the role of the two distances.

The one extra outcome. Triangle construction returned 0, 1, or 2 points. Circles add a fifth case: when the two circles are identical with the same positive radius (same center and radius), every point of the circle is a solution. A finite-point API cannot list infinitely many, so it must report this honestly — never as a plain "none." (Two identical zero-radius circles are the special exception: they are the same single point, so they share exactly one point, not infinitely many.)

🧠 2 — Intuition from Scratch

2.1 — A point on both circles is a triangle's third vertex

Suppose X lies on circle (A, r₁) and circle (B, r₂). Then |X − A| = r₁ and |X − B| = r₂. Those are precisely the two distance constraints of Class 05's triangleThirdPoint, with base A–B. The triangle's base length is the center distance d = |A − B|, and its two side lengths are the radii.

Diagram · An intersection point is the third vertex of triangle A–B–X

2.2 — Sliding one circle past the other

Hold circle A fixed and slide circle B in from far away. At first they are too far apart and never touch (disjoint). As d shrinks to exactly r₁ + r₂ they kiss at one point (externally tangent). Closer still, they overlap and cross at two points. Keep going and they kiss again from the inside at d = |r₁ − r₂| (internally tangent), and once d falls below that, the smaller sits entirely inside the larger with no contact (nested). The whole story is told by comparing d against r₁ + r₂ and |r₁ − r₂|.

👁 3 — Visual Mental Model

3.1 — The five configurations

Every pair of distinct-center circles is exactly one of these. The sixth row covers the special same-center cases.

Two intersection points (d < r₁+r₂, d > |r₁−r₂|)
Externally tangent (d = r₁ + r₂)
Internally tangent (d = |r₁ − r₂|)
Disjoint (d > r₁ + r₂)
One inside the other (d < |r₁ − r₂|)
Coincident (same center & same positive radius) — infinitely many
Two same-center cases, two different verdicts. If two circles share a center but have different radii, they never meet (a special nested case). If they share a center and the same positive radius, they are the same circle and meet everywhere (infinitely many). The lone exception is when both radii are zero: the two "circles" are the same single point, so they share exactly one point. All of these have d = 0, so the radius comparison is what separates them.

🧮 4 — Formal Abstraction

4.1 — Classification by center distance

Let d = |A − B| be the distance between centers and r₁, r₂ the radii. With distinct centers (d > 0):

ConditionConfigurationIntersections
d > r₁ + r₂Disjoint (apart)0
d = r₁ + r₂Externally tangent1
|r₁ − r₂| < d < r₁ + r₂Proper crossing2
d = |r₁ − r₂|Internally tangent1
d < |r₁ − r₂|One inside the other0

And the two same-center cases (d = 0): equal positive radii ⇒ coincident, infinitely many; both radii zero ⇒ one shared point; different radii ⇒ concentric, zero. (A negative radius is rejected as invalid before any of this.)

4.2 — Foot offset and half-chord (reusing Class 05)

Put the foot of the intersection chord on the center line. With base length d and side lengths r₁ (from A) and r₂ (from B), the Class 05 formulas give the foot offset from A and the half-chord height:

x = (r₁² − r₂² + d²) / (2d)        // distance from A to the chord's foot
h = sqrt(r₁² − x²)                  // half-chord; the two points are foot ± h·n

where n is the unit normal to the center line. The radicand r₁² − x² ≥ 0 is exactly the "feasible" condition — and it matches the distance classification above.

4.3 — Tangent length from an external point

For a point P outside a circle of center O and radius r, the tangent segment from P to its touch point is the leg of a right triangle whose hypotenuse is |P − O| and whose other leg is r (the radius is perpendicular to the tangent at the touch point). Hence

tangentLength = sqrt(|P − O|² − r²)

💡 5 — Key Observations

  • O1 — Intersection is the third-vertex construction. A shared point is at distance r₁ from A and r₂ from B, so circleCircleIntersect is a thin wrapper over triangleThirdPoint.
  • O2 — Classify before constructing. The distance test (§4.1) names the configuration up front, so the construction step never has to guess.
  • O3 — Zero and infinity are different answers sharing one code. Disjoint and coincident both yield "no finite point list," but they mean opposite things; the API must say which.
  • O4 — Tangency is the boundary. The single-point cases sit exactly where the half-chord height collapses to zero, i.e. d = r₁ + r₂ or d = |r₁ − r₂|.
  • O5 — Circle-line is the same chord idea. Drop the center onto the line, get the distance from center to line, then the half-chord is sqrt(r² − dist²) — no triangle needed.
  • O6 — Tangent points are a circle-circle intersection in disguise. The two touch points lie on the original circle and on the circle centered at P with radius equal to the tangent length.
  • O7 — EPS decides the boundary. Whether a near-tangent counts as one point or two (or zero) is a cmp decision; the same tolerance must drive classification and construction.

⚙ 6 — Algorithm / Method Explanation

6.1 — Classifying two circles

  1. Compute d = |A − B|.
  2. If d = 0: equal positive radii ⇒ coincident (infinitely many); both radii zero ⇒ one shared point; else concentric (none).
  3. Otherwise compare d with r₁ + r₂ and with |r₁ − r₂|: beyond the sum or below the difference ⇒ none; equal to either ⇒ tangent (one); strictly between ⇒ two.
Diagram · Classification decision flow on the center distance d

6.2 — Recovering the intersection points

Once classified as tangent or crossing, call triangleThirdPoint(A, B, r₁, r₂, +1, found) for one point and, if found = 2, again with −1 for its mirror across the center line. Coincident positive-radius circles are intercepted before this step and reported as "infinitely many"; identical zero-radius point-circles are intercepted as a single shared point.

Diagram · Center–center base with foot and perpendicular half-chord

6.3 — Circle versus line

Parameterize the line through P and Q as X(t) = P + d·t with d = Q − P. Project the center O onto the line: the foot parameter is t₀ = dot(O − P, d) / dot(d, d), giving the foot F = P + d·t₀. Let D be the distance from O to F. If D > r the line misses; if D = r it is tangent and the only parameter is t₁ = t₂ = t₀; otherwise the half-chord length is h = √(r² − D²).

Because moving by Δt along the parameter moves |d|·Δt in real distance, the parameter offset that covers the half-chord is delta = h / |d|. Therefore t₁ = t₀ + delta and t₂ = t₀ − delta, with points I₁ = P + d·t₁ and I₂ = P + d·t₂. The routine returns intersections on the infinite line; the caller uses t₁/t₂ to filter for the segment or ray variants.

How to interpret t for segment and ray versions

Condition on tMeaning
all real tpoint lies on the infinite line
0 ≤ t ≤ 1point lies on segment [P, Q]
t = 0point is exactly P
t = 1point is exactly Q
t < 0point is behind P, on the opposite extension
t > 1point is beyond Q in the P → Q direction
t ≥ 0point lies on the ray starting at P and going through Q
t ≤ 1point lies on the ray starting at Q and going through P, using the same P → Q parameter

6.4 — Tangents from an external point

If P is strictly inside, there is no tangent. If on the circle, the tangent touches at P. If outside, the tangent length is L = sqrt(|P − O|² − r²), and the two touch points are where the original circle meets the circle centered at P of radius L — a circle-circle intersection.

Diagram · Tangents from external point P: right triangle O–T–P

🔒 7 — Invariants

State invariants

  • I1 — Classification is total and exclusive. classifyCircles returns exactly one of {−3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2}: invalid radius, coincident non-zero circle (infinitely many), concentric-none, disjoint/nested-none, tangent or identical zero-radius point-circles, crossing.
  • I2 — The intersection wrapper never invents points. circleCircleIntersect returns 1 only when there is exactly one finite shared point: either a tangent point or identical zero-radius point-circles; it returns 2 only when genuinely crossing. Any returned point lies on both circles up to tolerance.
  • I3 — Zero is overloaded but documented. A return of 0 means "no finite unique output": none, an invalid radius, or the coincident-∞ case. The distinct semantics live in classifyCircles (the −3 invalid and −2 coincident codes) so callers who need the difference can ask. The identical zero-radius point-circle case is the exception that returns 1 with the shared point, never 0.

Transition invariants

  • I4 — Tangent points sit on both circles. For tangentPointsFromExternal, each returned T satisfies |T − O| = r and (T − O) ⟂ (T − P) up to tolerance.
  • I5 — Classification agrees with construction. circleCircleIntersect returns 1/2 exactly when classifyCircles returns 1/2, and 0 for every other class.

Termination invariants

Every routine is straight-line arithmetic with no loops or recursion. circleCircleIntersect may delegate to triangleThirdPoint twice to obtain the two mirror intersections; all other construction routines use at most one such construction step, so each routine still runs in O(1).

Failure mode — preconditions. Radii must be non-negative; classifyCircles reports −3 for a negative radius, while finite-output wrappers return 0 and the training drill prints INVALID. The circle-line routine needs a genuine line (P ≠ Q). The external-tangent routine assumes P is not strictly inside; it reports 0 if it is.

📜 8 — Proof of Correctness

8.1 — Why the distance test classifies correctly

Fix circle A and consider the closest and farthest points of circle B from A's center, which lie at distances d − r₂ and d + r₂. Circle A (radius r₁) meets circle B iff r₁ falls within that closed range, i.e. |d − r₂| ≤ r₁ ≤ d + r₂. Rearranging the right inequality gives d ≤ r₁ + r₂; the left gives d ≥ |r₁ − r₂|. Equality on either side is a single tangency; strict on both is a two-point crossing; violation of either is emptiness. This is exactly the table of §4.1.

8.2 — The chord foot and half-chord

This is the Class 05 derivation with base length d and side lengths r₁, r₂. The two right triangles sharing the half-chord height give r₁² = x² + h² and r₂² = (d − x)² + h²; subtracting eliminates h² and yields x = (r₁² − r₂² + d²)/(2d), then h = √(r₁² − x²). The radicand is non-negative exactly on the meeting range of §8.1, so feasibility of the construction and the distance classification coincide.

8.3 — Circle-line half-chord

Let F be the foot of the perpendicular from the center O to the line, and D = |O − F|. Any point on the line at signed offset s from F (along the unit direction) is at distance √(D² + s²) from O. Setting this equal to r gives s² = r² − D²: no solution when D > r, the single point F when D = r, and the symmetric pair F ± √(r² − D²)·û otherwise.

To recover the line parameters, write the line as X(t) = P + d·t with d = Q − P. The projection foot has parameter t₀ = dot(O − P, d) / dot(d, d), so F = P + d·t₀. An intersection offset by parameter delta from the foot is X = P + d·(t₀ ± delta), and its real distance from the foot is |d|·|delta|. The right triangle with legs |d|·delta and D and hypotenuse r gives

(|d| * delta)^2 + D^2 = r^2
⇒  delta = sqrt(r^2 - D^2) / |d|

Therefore t₁ = t₀ + delta and t₂ = t₀ − delta, and the returned points are I₁ = P + d·t₁ and I₂ = P + d·t₂. At tangency delta = 0, so t₁ = t₂ = t₀ and both points collapse to the foot.

Diagram · Circle–line chord: foot F, center distance D, half-chord √(r²−D²)

8.4 — Tangent length and the right angle

At a tangent touch point T, the radius OT is perpendicular to the tangent line PT (a radius meets its tangent at a right angle). Triangle O–T–P therefore has a right angle at T, with hypotenuse |O − P| and legs r and L. Pythagoras gives L² = |O − P|² − r², the tangent length. The touch points themselves are at distance r from O and L from P — a circle-circle intersection of (O, r) with (P, L), which is why the tangent-point routine reuses the intersection engine.

8.5 — Distinguishing zero from infinity

When d = 0, the construction's base collapses and the chord direction is undefined. If the radii differ, no point can be at two different distances from the same center, so the solution set is empty. If the radii are equal and positive, every point of the common circle satisfies both constraints, so the solution set is the whole circle. A finite-point routine cannot enumerate the latter, so it returns the same numeric 0 as the empty case — but the classifier exposes −2 versus −1 so the two are never genuinely conflated. The one remaining sub-case is equal radii that are both zero: then each "circle" is a single point, and the two coincide at exactly one shared point, so the classifier returns 1 and the intersection routine yields that point — not infinitely many, and not none.

📐 10 — Core Template Philosophy

  • Classify, then construct. Decide the configuration from distances first; only then build points. The classifier is the single source of truth for "how many."
  • Reuse the third-vertex engine. Do not re-derive circle intersection — feed centers as the base and radii as the side lengths into triangleThirdPoint.
  • Never conflate 0 with ∞. If one numeric code must cover both, document the difference and expose the real classification (the −2 coincident code) for callers who care.
  • Non-negative radii, stated and checked. A negative radius is meaningless; the classifier rejects it with −3; finite-output wrappers map invalid input to 0.
  • One tolerance everywhere. The same cmp/EPS drives both classification and construction, so the boundary verdict is consistent.

💻 11 — Master / Base Implementation

Standalone, compiles with g++ -std=c++17. The first block repeats just enough of the Class 01–05 substrate (including the hardened triangleThirdPoint) for the file to build on its own; the Class 06 additions follow the divider.

#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;

using T     = long double;
using Point = complex<T>;

#define X real()
#define Y imag()

const long double EPS = 1e-12L;
const T PI = acosl(-1.0L);

// ---- rotation helper (typed function, not a macro) ----------------
Point rot(const Point& v, T t) { return v * polar((T)1, t); }

// ---- Class 01-05 substrate ----------------------------------------
template<class U> int cmpInt(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) { return (a > b) - (a < b); }
template<class U> int cmpFloat(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    long double d = (long double)a - (long double)b;
    return (d > EPS) - (d < -EPS);
}
template<class U> int cmp(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    if constexpr (is_floating_point_v<U>) return cmpFloat(a, b);
    else return cmpInt(a, b);
}
T safeSqrt(T x) { if (cmp(x) == 0) return 0; return (T)sqrtl((long double)x); }
T dot  (const Point& a, const Point& b) { return real(conj(a) * b); }
T cross(const Point& a, const Point& b) { return imag(conj(a) * b); }
T len (const Point& a) { return (T)hypotl((long double)a.X, (long double)a.Y); }
T dist(const Point& a, const Point& b) { return len(a - b); }
bool samePoint(const Point& a, const Point& b) {
    return cmp(a.X, b.X) == 0 && cmp(a.Y, b.Y) == 0;
}
Point perpCCW(const Point& v) { return Point(-v.Y, v.X); }   // left normal
int orient(const Point& a, const Point& b, const Point& p) {
    return cmp(cross(b - a, p - a));
}

// Class 05 third-vertex engine (hardened).
//   found: 0 no unique point, 1 single point, 2 two mirror points.
Point triangleThirdPoint(const Point& A, const Point& B,
                         T b, T a, int side, int& found) {
    if (cmp(a) < 0 || cmp(b) < 0) { found = 0; return A; }   // negative length
    if (side != +1 && side != -1) { found = 0; return A; }    // invalid side flag
    T c = dist(A, B);
    if (cmp(c) == 0) {                                        // coincident base
        if (cmp(a) == 0 && cmp(b) == 0) { found = 1; return A; }
        found = 0; return A;
    }
    T x  = (b*b - a*a + c*c) / (2*c);
    T h2 = b*b - x*x;
    if (cmp(h2) < 0) { found = 0; return A; }
    T h = safeSqrt(h2);
    Point dir  = (B - A) / c;
    Point foot = A + dir * x;
    if (cmp(h) == 0) { found = 1; return foot; }
    found = 2;
    return foot + perpCCW(dir) * (h * (T)side);
}

// ===================================================================
// CLASS 06 — circle intersection & tangents
// ===================================================================

struct Circle { Point c; T r; };

// Classify two circles by center distance d and radii.
//   -3 -> invalid radius
//   -2 -> coincident non-zero-radius circles: infinitely many shared points
//   -1 -> concentric, different radii: no intersection
//    0 -> disjoint (apart) or strictly nested: no intersection
//    1 -> tangent or identical zero-radius point-circles: one point
//    2 -> proper crossing: two points
int classifyCircles(const Circle& A, const Circle& B) {
    if (cmp(A.r) < 0 || cmp(B.r) < 0) return -3;   // invalid radius

    T d = dist(A.c, B.c);
    if (cmp(d) == 0) {                         // same center
        if (cmp(A.r, B.r) == 0) {
            if (cmp(A.r) == 0) return 1;       // same zero-radius point-circle -> one point
            return -2;                         // identical real circle -> infinite
        }
        return -1;                             // concentric, different radii
    }
    int outer = cmp(d, A.r + B.r);                          // d vs (r1 + r2)
    int inner = cmp(d, (T)fabsl((long double)(A.r - B.r))); // d vs |r1 - r2|
    if (outer > 0) return 0;                   // too far apart
    if (inner < 0) return 0;                   // one strictly inside the other
    if (outer == 0 || inner == 0) return 1;    // tangent (external or internal)
    return 2;                                  // two crossings
}

// Circle-circle intersection points.
// returns:
//   0 -> no finite unique output from this wrapper: none (disjoint / nested /
//        concentric), invalid radius, OR coincident non-zero circles with
//        infinitely many intersections (classifyCircles == -2). Use
//        classifyCircles to tell these apart.
//   1 -> tangent, OR identical zero-radius point-circles: a single point in P1.
//   2 -> two intersection points P1, P2.
// Preconditions: radii are non-negative.
int circleCircleIntersect(const Circle& A, const Circle& B, Point& P1, Point& P2) {
    if (cmp(A.r) < 0 || cmp(B.r) < 0) return 0;   // invalid radius
    int cls = classifyCircles(A, B);
    if (cls == -3) return 0;   // invalid radius
    if (cls == 1 && samePoint(A.c, B.c) && cmp(A.r) == 0 && cmp(B.r) == 0) {
        P1 = A.c;              // identical zero-radius point-circles: one shared point
        return 1;
    }
    if (cls == -2) return 0;   // infinitely many: no finite unique output
    if (cls <= 0)  return 0;   // -1 concentric or 0 disjoint/nested: none
    // A shared point is at distance A.r from A.c and B.r from B.c: the third
    // vertex of triangle (A.c, B.c) with side lengths (A.r, B.r). Reuse Class 05.
    int found;
    P1 = triangleThirdPoint(A.c, B.c, A.r, B.r, +1, found);
    if (found == 1) return 1;                  // tangent: single point
    if (found == 2) {
        P2 = triangleThirdPoint(A.c, B.c, A.r, B.r, -1, found);
        return 2;
    }
    return 0;                                   // defensive (classify already screened)
}

// Circle-line intersection with parameter exposure.
// The infinite line is parameterized as X(t) = P + (Q - P) * t.
// returns:
//   0 -> no intersection or invalid input; t1/t2 unused
//   1 -> tangent: single point I1 with t1 = t2
//   2 -> two points I1/I2 with parameters t1/t2
// Parameter meaning:
//   t = 0 at P, t = 1 at Q
//   0 <= t <= 1 -> on segment [P,Q]
//   t >= 0      -> on ray P -> Q
//   t < 0       -> on the opposite extension behind P
//   t > 1       -> beyond Q in the P -> Q direction
// Preconditions: r >= 0, P != Q.
int circleLineIntersect(const Circle& C, const Point& P, const Point& Q,
                        Point& I1, Point& I2, T& t1, T& t2) {
    t1 = t2 = 0;                                  // unused when return value is 0
    if (cmp(C.r) < 0) return 0;
    Point d = Q - P;
    T dd = dot(d, d);
    if (cmp(dd) == 0) return 0;                   // P == Q is not a line

    T t0 = dot(C.c - P, d) / dd;                  // foot parameter on P + d*t
    Point foot = P + d * t0;

    T D2 = dot(C.c - foot, C.c - foot);           // squared center-to-line distance
    T h2 = C.r * C.r - D2;                         // squared half-chord
    if (cmp(h2) < 0) return 0;                     // line misses the circle

    if (cmp(h2) == 0) {
        t1 = t2 = t0;
        I1 = foot;
        return 1;
    }

    T h = safeSqrt(h2);
    T delta = h / len(d);                          // |d| * delta = h

    t1 = t0 + delta;
    t2 = t0 - delta;

    I1 = P + d * t1;
    I2 = P + d * t2;
    return 2;
}

// Tangent length from external point P to circle C: sqrt(|P - O|^2 - r^2).
// Precondition: P outside or on C (else the radicand is negative), r >= 0.
T tangentLength(const Circle& C, const Point& P) {
    T d2 = dot(P - C.c, P - C.c);
    return safeSqrt(d2 - C.r * C.r);
}

// Tangent touch points on circle C from external point P.
// returns: 0 P strictly inside (no tangent), 1 P on the circle (touch at P),
//          2 two touch points T1, T2. Precondition: r >= 0.
int tangentPointsFromExternal(const Circle& C, const Point& P, Point& T1, Point& T2) {
    if (cmp(C.r) < 0) return 0;
    T d = dist(P, C.c);
    int s = cmp(d, C.r);
    if (s < 0) return 0;                          // P strictly inside: no tangent
    if (s == 0) { T1 = P; return 1; }            // P on the circle: tangent at P
    // Touch points are on C (distance r from O) and at distance L from P:
    // a circle-circle intersection of (O, r) with (P, L).
    T L = tangentLength(C, P);
    Circle around{P, L};
    return circleCircleIntersect(C, around, T1, T2);
}

// ===================================================================
// Recommended vector-return APIs (preferred for new CP code).
// They are thin wrappers over the count/output-parameter functions
// above and never throw; an empty vector means "no finite answer".
// ===================================================================

// Returns 0, 1, or 2 intersection points of two circles. The +1 side
// (CCW from A.c -> B.c) is listed first when two are returned.
vector<Point> circleCircleIntersection(const Circle& A, const Circle& B) {
    Point P1, P2;
    int k = circleCircleIntersect(A, B, P1, P2);
    if (k == 0) return {};
    if (k == 1) return { P1 };
    return { P1, P2 };
}

// Returns the touch points of the tangents from an external point P to
// circle C. Size 0 if P is strictly inside C, size 1 if P is on C,
// size 2 if P is strictly outside.
vector<Point> tangentsFromPointToCircle(const Circle& C, const Point& P) {
    Point T1, T2;
    int k = tangentPointsFromExternal(C, P, T1, T2);
    if (k == 0) return {};
    if (k == 1) return { T1 };
    return { T1, T2 };
}

// Common tangent lines between two circles, returned as endpoint pairs
// (touch on A, touch on B). Sign s = +1 builds the external tangents
// (touches on the same side), s = -1 the internal tangents (opposite
// sides). The shared engine is the same triangle3rd construction used
// for circle-circle intersection: we look for a unit normal n with
// |n| = 1 and dot(d, n) = R = A.r - s * B.r, where d = B.c - A.c.
vector<pair<Point,Point>> circleCircleTangents(const Circle& A, const Circle& B) {
    vector<pair<Point,Point>> ans;
    if (cmp(A.r) < 0 || cmp(B.r) < 0) return ans;
    Point d = B.c - A.c;
    T d2 = dot(d, d);
    if (cmp(d2) == 0) return ans;                 // concentric: skip (degenerate set)

    for (int s : {1, -1}) {
        T R  = A.r - s * B.r;
        T rr = d2 + 1 - 2 * R;                    // |n - d|^2 when |n| = 1 and dot(d,n) = R
        if (cmp(rr) < 0) continue;
        // Reuse the third-vertex engine: feed origin and d as the base,
        // |n| = 1 and |n - d| = sqrt(rr) as the two side lengths.
        T h = safeSqrt(rr);
        int found;
        Point n1 = triangleThirdPoint(Point(0,0), d, (T)1, h, +1, found);
        if (found >= 1) {
            Point P = A.c + n1 * A.r;
            Point Q = B.c + n1 * (s * B.r);
            ans.push_back({P, Q});
        }
        if (found == 2) {
            Point n2 = triangleThirdPoint(Point(0,0), d, (T)1, h, -1, found);
            Point P = A.c + n2 * A.r;
            Point Q = B.c + n2 * (s * B.r);
            ans.push_back({P, Q});
        }
    }
    return ans;
}
Two macros that look identical to fields but are not. #define X real() makes v.X expand to v.real(); #define Y imag() likewise. rot is a typed helper function, never a macro — and never named rotate (which collides with std::rotate).

⚠ 14 — Common Pitfalls / Bugs

  • P1 — Reporting coincident positive-radius circles as "no intersection." The biggest trap: d = 0 with equal positive radii means infinitely many points, not zero; equal zero radii means exactly one shared point. Intercept these before the finite construction and label them honestly — the same numeric 0 as "empty" needs a different documented meaning.
  • P2 — Forgetting the internal-tangent and nested cases. Checking only d > r₁ + r₂ misses the small-circle-inside-large-circle family. Always test |r₁ − r₂| too.
  • P3 — Using raw </== on distances. The tangent boundary is exact only with cmp/EPS; a raw comparison flickers between one and two points on near-tangent inputs.
  • P4 — sqrt of a negative half-chord. When the circles miss or the line misses, the radicand is negative. Test its sign first; safeSqrt guards rounding noise but the miss must be detected before constructing.
  • P5 — Negative or zero radius mishandled. Reject negatives; treat a zero radius as a point and let the distance test classify it, rather than special-casing it incorrectly.
  • P6 — Computing tangent length when P is inside. Then |P − O|² − r² < 0 and no real tangent exists. Check P is outside (or on) the circle first.
  • P7 — Treating a degenerate "line" P = Q as a line. The direction is zero and the projection divides by zero. Require P ≠ Q.

Reflex rules (contest speed)

Two circles meet ⟺ |r₁−r₂| ≤ d ≤ r₁+r₂. Tangent ⟺ d = r₁+r₂ (out) or d = |r₁−r₂| (in). Foot: x = (r₁²−r₂²+d²)/(2d); chord half = √(r₁²−x²). d = 0: equal positive r ⇒ ∞; both r = 0 ⇒ one point; unequal r ⇒ none. Tangent length = √(|P−O|²−r²). Never call 0 and ∞ the same answer.

Common confusion

"My intersection routine says two identical circles don't meet." Two identical circles with the same positive radius meet everywhere — the routine just cannot list infinitely many finite points, so it returns 0 as "no finite unique output." Call classifyCircles and check for the −2 coincident code to detect this case explicitly. (Two identical zero-radius circles are the exception: they coincide at one point, classified as 1.)

🌿 16 — Variants & Extensions

16.1 — The tangent line directions (external tangent angle)

Once the two touch points are known from tangentPointsFromExternal, the tangent lines are simply the lines through P and each touch point. What changes: you emit lines, not points. Why: many problems want the tangent direction or the angle subtended. When to use: visibility, wrapping, and "cone from a point" problems.

// Tangent lines from external P to circle C, as (P, touchPoint) pairs.
// Returns the number of distinct tangents through the touch points (0, 1, or 2).
int tangentLinesFromExternal(const Circle& C, const Point& P,
                             Point& touch1, Point& touch2) {
    return tangentPointsFromExternal(C, P, touch1, touch2);
    // each line is simply the line through P and the corresponding touch point
}

16.2 — Internal vs external tangents between two circles

Two separate circles share up to four common tangent lines: two external (both circles on the same side) and two internal (circles on opposite sides, crossing between them). The count drops as the circles approach: four when disjoint, three at external tangency, two when crossing, one at internal tangency, none when nested. The construction reduces to tangents from a point after shrinking one circle and growing the other by the same radius (a standard reduction), which lands back on tangentPointsFromExternal.

16.3 — Radical axis (when you only need the chord line)

For two non-concentric circles, the set of points with equal tangent length to both is a line — the radical axis — perpendicular to the center line. When the circles cross, it is exactly the line through the two intersection points. When to use: problems that need the common chord's line without the points, or power-of-a-point arguments.

// Signed offset (from A.c, along the unit A->B direction) of the radical axis.
// Precondition: distinct centers (d > 0).
T radicalAxisOffset(const Circle& A, const Circle& B) {
    T d = dist(A.c, B.c);
    return (A.r*A.r - B.r*B.r + d*d) / (2*d);   // same x as the chord foot
}

16.4 — Recommended vector-return APIs (clean copy-paste form)

For new contest code, prefer the vector-return wrappers defined alongside the master implementation. They never throw, they never use sentinel pointers, and every possible outcome (0 / 1 / 2 results) flows out of the same call as a vector size. The classic count + output-parameter forms (circleCircleIntersect, tangentPointsFromExternal) remain available as defensive variants for callers who already track a found integer or write to pre-allocated buffers.

// 0, 1, or 2 intersection points (CCW side first when two).
vector<Point> circleCircleIntersection(const Circle& A, const Circle& B);

// 0, 1, or 2 touch points of tangents from an external point P to circle C.
vector<Point> tangentsFromPointToCircle(const Circle& C, const Point& P);

// Common tangents between two circles as endpoint pairs (touch on A, touch on B).
// External tangents come first, then internal; total 0..4 depending on configuration.
vector<pair<Point,Point>> circleCircleTangents(const Circle& A, const Circle& B);

When to use which. Reach for the vector-return form by default; reach for the count + output-parameter form when you genuinely care about avoiding any heap allocation in a hot inner loop (e.g. sweeping millions of pairs).

🚀 17 — Problem-Based Applications & Training Drills

Training drill — intersect two circles, reporting 0 / 1 / 2 / ∞ the canonical classification pattern, not a contest problem

Scope. This is a training drill for circle-circle intersection, not a full contest problem. It isolates the classify-then-construct pattern and, crucially, the honest reporting of the coincident (infinite) case.

Story. Given two circles by center and radius, report how they meet: their two intersection points, their single tangent point, that they do not meet, or that they are the same positive-radius circle (infinitely many points).

Abstraction. Call classifyCircles for the verdict; for the tangent and crossing cases, recover the points with circleCircleIntersect.

Why this geometry model fits. A shared point is the third vertex of a triangle on the centers with the radii as side lengths (§8.2), so the construction is exact and the distance test (§8.1) names the configuration.

Algorithm. Read both circles; classify; print INFINITE for coincident positive-radius circles, NONE for empty, the single point for tangent, or both points for a crossing.

#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;

using T     = long double;
using Point = complex<T>;
#define X real()
#define Y imag()

const long double EPS = 1e-12L;

template<class U> int cmpFloat(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    long double d = (long double)a - (long double)b;
    return (d > EPS) - (d < -EPS);
}
template<class U> int cmp(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    if constexpr (is_floating_point_v<U>) return cmpFloat(a, b);
    else return (a > b) - (a < b);
}
T safeSqrt(T x) { if (cmp(x) == 0) return 0; return (T)sqrtl((long double)x); }
T len (const Point& a) { return (T)hypotl((long double)a.X, (long double)a.Y); }
T dist(const Point& a, const Point& b) { return len(a - b); }
Point perpCCW(const Point& v) { return Point(-v.Y, v.X); }

struct Circle { Point c; T r; };

// -3 invalid radius, -2 coincident(inf), -1 concentric(none), 0 disjoint/nested(none),
//  1 tangent or identical zero-radius point-circles, 2 crossing
int classifyCircles(const Circle& A, const Circle& B) {
    if (cmp(A.r) < 0 || cmp(B.r) < 0) return -3;

    T d = dist(A.c, B.c);
    if (cmp(d) == 0) {
        if (cmp(A.r, B.r) == 0) {
            if (cmp(A.r) == 0) return 1;          // same zero-radius point-circle
            return -2;                            // same positive-radius circle
        }
        return -1;
    }
    int outer = cmp(d, A.r + B.r);
    int inner = cmp(d, (T)fabsl((long double)(A.r - B.r)));
    if (outer > 0 || inner < 0) return 0;
    if (outer == 0 || inner == 0) return 1;
    return 2;
}

// returns the intersection points for the tangent/crossing classes.
// found: 1 single point P1, 2 points P1 & P2. (Caller handles 0 / infinite.)
int twoCirclePoints(const Circle& A, const Circle& B, Point& P1, Point& P2) {
    T d = dist(A.c, B.c);
    if (cmp(d) == 0 && cmp(A.r) == 0 && cmp(B.r) == 0) {  // identical zero-radius point-circles
        P1 = A.c;
        return 1;
    }
    T x  = (A.r*A.r - B.r*B.r + d*d) / (2*d);
    T h2 = A.r*A.r - x*x;
    Point dir  = (B.c - A.c) / d;
    Point foot = A.c + dir * x;
    if (cmp(h2) == 0) { P1 = foot; return 1; }
    T h = safeSqrt(h2);
    Point nrm = perpCCW(dir);
    P1 = foot + nrm * h;
    P2 = foot - nrm * h;
    return 2;
}

int main() {
    ios::sync_with_stdio(false);
    cin.tie(nullptr);

    long double ax, ay, ar, bx, by, br;
    while (cin >> ax >> ay >> ar >> bx >> by >> br) {
        Circle A{Point(ax, ay), ar}, B{Point(bx, by), br};
        if (cmp(ar) < 0 || cmp(br) < 0) { cout << "INVALID\n"; continue; }
        int cls = classifyCircles(A, B);
        if (cls == -3) { cout << "INVALID\n";  continue; }
        if (cls == -2) { cout << "INFINITE\n"; continue; }
        if (cls <= 0)  { cout << "NONE\n";     continue; }
        Point P1, P2;
        int k = twoCirclePoints(A, B, P1, P2);
        cout << fixed << setprecision(6);
        if (k == 1) cout << "TANGENT " << P1.X << ' ' << P1.Y << '\n';
        else        cout << "TWO " << P1.X << ' ' << P1.Y << ' '
                         << P2.X << ' ' << P2.Y << '\n';
    }
    return 0;
}

Complexity. O(1) per query.

Output meaning. INFINITE is the coincident case — same center and same positive radius — distinct from NONE, which is genuinely empty (disjoint, nested, or concentric). TANGENT also covers the identical zero-radius point-circle case (both radii zero, same center), which has exactly one shared point. INVALID flags a negative radius. Never collapse these into one label.

Pitfalls. Compare distances with cmp, not raw operators, so tangency lands exactly. Guard the coincident case before dividing by d.

Training drill — tangent length from a point to a circle right-triangle pattern, not a contest problem

Scope. A pattern drill for the tangent-length right triangle, not a standalone contest problem. It shows the √(|P−O|² − r²) computation and its precondition.

Story. Given a circle and an external point, output the length of the tangent segment from the point to the circle, or report that no tangent exists when the point is inside.

Abstraction. This is tangentLength, guarded by a check that the point is outside or on the circle.

Why this geometry model fits. The radius meets the tangent at a right angle (§8.4), so the tangent length is a Pythagorean leg of triangle O–T–P.

Algorithm. Compare |P − O| with r; if outside or on, print √(|P−O|² − r²); otherwise report no tangent.

#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;

using T     = long double;
using Point = complex<T>;
#define X real()
#define Y imag()

const long double EPS = 1e-12L;

template<class U> int cmpFloat(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    long double d = (long double)a - (long double)b;
    return (d > EPS) - (d < -EPS);
}
template<class U> int cmp(const U& a, const U& b = U(0)) {
    if constexpr (is_floating_point_v<U>) return cmpFloat(a, b);
    else return (a > b) - (a < b);
}
T safeSqrt(T x) { if (cmp(x) == 0) return 0; return (T)sqrtl((long double)x); }
T dot (const Point& a, const Point& b) { return real(conj(a) * b); }
T len (const Point& a) { return (T)hypotl((long double)a.X, (long double)a.Y); }
T dist(const Point& a, const Point& b) { return len(a - b); }

int main() {
    ios::sync_with_stdio(false);
    cin.tie(nullptr);

    long double ox, oy, r, px, py;
    while (cin >> ox >> oy >> r >> px >> py) {
        Point O(ox, oy), P(px, py);
        if (cmp(r) < 0) { cout << "INVALID\n"; continue; }
        int s = cmp(dist(P, O), r);
        if (s < 0) { cout << "INSIDE_NO_TANGENT\n"; continue; }
        T d2 = dot(P - O, P - O);
        cout << fixed << setprecision(6)
             << safeSqrt(d2 - r * r) << '\n';   // 0 exactly when P is on the circle
    }
    return 0;
}

Complexity. O(1) per query.

Pitfalls. The radicand |P−O|² − r² is negative when P is strictly inside — screen that first. A point exactly on the circle gives length 0, which is correct.

📝 18 — Final Summary

The mental model

  • An intersection point is a triangle's third vertex on the centers.
  • Classify by distance: compare d with r₁+r₂ and |r₁−r₂|.
  • Foot: x = (r₁²−r₂²+d²)/(2d); half-chord √(r₁²−x²).
  • Circle-line: half-chord √(r²−D²) off the foot.
  • Tangent length: √(|P−O|²−r²).
  • Tangent points: circle-circle intersection with radius L.

The hard rules

  • 0, 1, 2, or ∞ — circles add the infinite case.
  • Coincident (same positive radius) ≠ disjoint; document the shared 0 code. Both zero-radius ⇒ one point.
  • Classify before you construct.
  • Test |r₁−r₂|, not just r₁+r₂.
  • Non-negative radii; cmp at every boundary.

Class 06 turns the Class 05 third-vertex engine into a full circle toolkit: classify two circles, recover their meeting points, cut a circle with a line, and reach tangents from an external point — always distinguishing "no points" from "infinitely many." These primitives feed the circle-cover and smallest-enclosing-circle work of Class 07.

What's next. Class 07 builds on circle construction toward the smallest enclosing circle (Welzl), maximum circle cover, and the ellipse utilities — where deciding whether points fit inside a circle leans directly on the distance and intersection tests established here.

📮 19 — Footer / Contact

Runtime Validation Checklist

Before using these circle and tangent routines in a contest solution, verify each item below. The Class 01 numeric checklist and the Class 05 construction checklist still apply underneath.

Classification & reporting

  • Center distance compared against both r₁ + r₂ and |r₁ − r₂| via cmp.
  • The d = 0 case splits on radius: equal positive radii ⇒ coincident (∞); both zero ⇒ one shared point; different radii ⇒ concentric (none).
  • The coincident-∞ case is reported distinctly from the empty case — the recommended circleCircleIntersection returns an empty vector<Point>, and the legacy circleCircleIntersect returns numeric 0; classifyCircles exposes −2 when you need to distinguish "coincident" from "disjoint."
  • Identical zero-radius circles are treated as one shared point, not as coincident circles with infinitely many points.
  • Radii are non-negative; a negative radius reports −3 from the classifier and an empty vector (or numeric 0 in the legacy form) from the intersection wrapper.

Construction & tangents

  • Prefer the vector-return API for new code: circleCircleIntersection, tangentsFromPointToCircle, circleCircleTangents. The output-parameter variants stay available as defensive / low-level helpers.
  • Intersection points come from triangleThirdPoint with centers as the base and radii as the side lengths.
  • The circle-line half-chord r² − D² has its sign checked before safeSqrt, and the line is genuine (P ≠ Q).
  • Tangent length is taken only when P is outside or on the circle; tangent points reuse the circle-circle engine.
  • circleLineIntersect returns t₁/t₂ for X(t) = P + (Q−P)·t; segment filtering uses 0 ≤ t ≤ 1, ray P → Q uses t ≥ 0.
  • The opposite extension behind P uses t < 0; beyond Q uses t > 1. The tangent-line case sets t₁ = t₂.
