Splay Tree (Self-Adjusting BST)

A binary search tree with one rule: every accessed node is rotated up to the root. No height bookkeeping, no random priorities, no color bits — and yet the average cost over any long sequence of operations is O(log n) per operation. This tutorial builds the splay tree from zero, proves the amortized bound from first principles using the potential method (telescoping sums and all), and ships two production templates: key splay with frequency-counted duplicates, and implicit splay with split/merge over sequences.
Theme

📌 01 · Problem Definition

Maintain a dynamic ordered structure that supports the full menu of ordered-set operations — insertion, deletion, membership, k-th smallest, rank-of-value, predecessor, successor — and additionally adapts its shape to the access pattern: frequently used elements should become cheap to reach, and recently used elements should become cheaper to reach again. The structure must guarantee O(log n) per operation in an amortized sense, meaning that any sequence of m operations on a tree of n nodes runs in O((n + m) log n) total time, even though individual operations may take longer.

This is a strictly more ambitious goal than the balanced BSTs of tutorial 26 or the treap of tutorial 27. A balanced BST guarantees O(log n) worst case for every single operation but does not exploit access patterns; a freshly accessed node sits at exactly the same depth as the previously accessed one. A splay tree gives up the per-operation worst-case bound in exchange for self-adaptation: it actively restructures itself on every access so that hot elements migrate toward the root. The amortized bound replaces the worst-case bound, and the constants remain small enough to be competitive in practice.

🧠 02 · Intuition from Scratch

Tutorial 26 explained that a BST stores its keys so that an inorder traversal reads them in sorted order, that every operation walks one root-to-leaf path of length O(h), and that a rotation is a local rewiring that preserves the inorder. We start from those facts and ask a slightly different question. Suppose we just spent O(h) time finding a node deep in the tree. Is there any way to repay that work, so that future searches for the same node are cheaper? The splay tree's answer is yes: after every access, rotate the accessed node all the way up to the root. The next search for the same value finds it immediately, at depth zero, and the access costs O(1).

The one rule

Whenever a search, an insertion, or a deletion touches a node x, we follow up by repeatedly rotating x up the tree until x is the root. The sequence of rotations is called splaying x. The splay tree has no other bookkeeping: no balance factor like AVL, no color bit like Red-Black, no random priority like the treap. It chooses its shape purely as a side effect of past access patterns.

Why the obvious approach is wrong

A first guess might be: just keep doing single rotations to bring x up one level at a time, the way a treap rotates a fresh leaf up its access path. That works in the sense that x reaches the root, but it does not buy us anything new. The chain on the access path stays a chain after a sequence of single rotations, just with a different leaf at the top. The splay tree's special insight is that the way you rotate matters as much as the fact that you rotate. There are three rotation patterns — zig, zig-zig, and zig-zag — and one of them, zig-zig, actively shortens the chain. We will see in section 6 exactly how this works and in section 8 why this shortening is what makes the amortized bound go through.

Why this is worth learning even when treaps exist

Treaps already give us a beautifully simple expected O(log n) per operation. Why bother with splay trees? Three reasons. First, splay trees are deterministic; their guarantee does not depend on a random source, which matters in adversarial or reproducible settings. Second, splay trees exhibit working-set behavior: if the workload has only k hot elements at a time, splay accesses cost O(log k) on average rather than O(log n). Third, the splay machinery generalises into link-cut trees and top trees, which are the standard structures for dynamic-tree problems; learning splay trees here is a prerequisite for those later topics.

🎨 03 · Visual Mental Model

When we splay a node x, three local patterns can occur depending on where x sits relative to its parent and grandparent. The pattern is named after the shape that x, p, and g form in the tree: zig when x has a parent but no grandparent (so the splay step is a single rotation), zig-zig when x and p are children on the same side of their parents (both left, or both right), and zig-zag when x and p are children on opposite sides. Each pattern moves x exactly two levels up, except zig which moves x one level up to the root because x already had no grandparent.

Splaying a deep leaf brings it to the root through a sequence of two-level lifts

The stepper above shows the splay of the deepest leaf of a chain. Notice that after the splay, the tree is no longer a chain: the path that x walked up has been folded in on itself, and roughly half the nodes are now significantly shallower. This is the self-adjustment effect. Future searches into the affected region will be cheaper, paying back the cost of this particular splay. Section 8 quantifies this payback formally using a potential function.

📐 04 · Formal Abstraction

What the splay tree keeps and what it deliberately drops

A splay tree is a binary search tree, full stop. Every node has a key (or, for the implicit variant, an implicit position), two children, and a parent pointer, and the BST invariant holds at every quiescent moment between operations. The structure does not maintain a height field, a balance factor, a color bit, or a priority. Drop all of those from your mental model — they belong to AVL, Red-Black, and the treap respectively, not to the splay tree. The shape of the splay tree at any moment is whatever the access history has produced; there is no shape we are trying to enforce.

The two splay tree invariants

BST invariant: For every node u, every node v in subtree(u->ch[L]) has v.key < u.key every node v in subtree(u->ch[R]) has v.key > u.key Size invariant: For every node u, u.sz = sz(u->ch[L]) + (frq if multiset else 1) + sz(u->ch[R])

In the key-splay multiset version the per-node count is frq, so the size field aggregates frequencies rather than distinct node counts; this is exactly the same convention as the key treap of tutorial 27. In the implicit-splay sequence version, there are no duplicate keys to merge, so the size field counts nodes one per node and frq is absent.

The parent invariant

Splay trees rely heavily on parent pointers. Every non-root node must point at its parent, and every root must have its parent set to the sentinel (EMPTY in Template A, E in Template B). The rotation primitive and the splay loop both inspect parent pointers to choose which case to apply, so a broken parent pointer is one of the easiest ways to corrupt a splay tree. Section 14 makes this explicit as pitfall number two.

What we deliberately do not require

There is no requirement that the tree be balanced. Right after inserting 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 in sorted order, a treap would produce a roughly balanced tree (because the priorities scrambled the order), but a splay tree may briefly look like a chain. The chain disappears the moment we splay any node back to the root, but in between operations we cannot assume any height bound. The amortized analysis in section 8 will tolerate occasional bad shapes by charging their cleanup against subsequent cheap operations.

🔍 05 · Key Observations

Splay is the only mutator of shape
Insert, erase, find, count, kth, order_of_key — every public operation ends by splaying some node to the root. There is no rebalance routine that runs separately from splay. The whole correctness story is just BST descent plus splay.
Zig-zig is the magic step
Of the three splay patterns, zig-zig is the one that bends the access path. Doing two zigs in a row (instead of one zig-zig) would also move x up by two levels, but it would leave the chain shape intact. Zig-zig rotates the parent first and then x, which compresses the access path into a more balanced shape.
Splaying preserves inorder
A splay operation is a sequence of rotations, and a rotation preserves inorder (tutorial 26 proved this). So splaying preserves the BST invariant globally even though it changes which node sits at the root.
Insert ends with splay; erase ends with splay-then-join
Insert finds the BST leaf position, attaches a new node, then splays the new node to the root. Erase finds the doomed node, splays it to the root (so its left and right subtrees become exposed as the only two subtrees of the root), removes the root, and joins the two subtrees by splaying the maximum of the left subtree and attaching the right subtree to it.
A single operation can be slow
If we splay a deep node, the splay itself walks O(h) levels and so may cost O(h) which can be as bad as O(n) in the worst case. The amortized guarantee says this expensive splay is paid for by subsequent cheap operations, but the per-operation worst case is genuinely linear.

⚙️ 06 · Algorithm

The splay routine takes a single argument: the node x we want to lift to the root. We repeat one of three local cases until x reaches the root. Each case is named by the relationship between x, its parent p, and its grandparent g. We will describe each case once in detail, then trust the symmetry to handle the mirror.

Zig: when x's parent is the root

If p is the root, there is no grandparent. The splay step is a single rotation that brings x up over p. After the rotation, x is the new root and p is one of x's children — left if x was originally the right child, right if x was originally the left child. Zig only fires as the last step of a splay, because once it has run, x has no parent and the splay loop terminates. It executes at most once per splay operation.

Zig: a single rotation when the parent is the root

Zig-zig: when x and p are children on the same side

If x is a left child of p and p is a left child of g — or the mirror image with both right children — the splay step is two rotations performed in a specific order: first rotate p over g, then rotate x over p. After both rotations, x sits where g used to be, p is one of x's children, and g is one of p's grandchildren. The ordering of the two rotations is critical: rotating x twice in a row instead would also move x up two levels, but it would not bend the access path the way zig-zig does. Section 8 will show that this bend is precisely what makes the amortized analysis work.

Zig-zig: rotate p first, then x — the order matters

Zig-zag: when x and p are children on opposite sides

If x is a right child of p but p is a left child of g — or the mirror image — the splay step is two rotations performed in the opposite order from zig-zig: rotate x twice in a row. The first rotation lifts x over p; x now sits directly under g, on the same side p used to occupy. The second rotation lifts x over g. The final configuration has x at the position g used to occupy, with p and g as its two children.

Zig-zag: two consecutive rotations of x — also called a double rotation

The full splay loop in pseudocode

splay(x): while x is not the root: p = parent(x) g = parent(p) if g is null: # zig rotate(x) else if dir(p, x) == dir(g, p): # zig-zig (same side) rotate(p) rotate(x) else: # zig-zag (opposite sides) rotate(x) rotate(x)

How public operations end with splay

Every public operation in the splay tree concludes by calling splay on a node that the operation has just visited. Insert splays the freshly inserted (or freshly bumped) node. Erase splays the doomed node to the root before removing it, then splays the maximum of the left subtree as part of joining the two surviving pieces. Find splays the matching node if one exists, or the last node touched during the descent if not. kth and order_of_key splay the located node on the way back. This splay-after-every-touch discipline is what activates the amortized guarantee; skipping splay on any path lets the chain re-emerge and breaks the bound.

📊 07 · Invariants and Transitions

State invariants between operations

Between public operations, the splay tree maintains the two invariants of section 4: the BST invariant on values and the size invariant on subtree sizes. Additionally, every non-root node points at its true parent and every root points at the sentinel (EMPTY in Template A, E in Template B). There is no third invariant about balance, height, or color — the tree's shape is whatever the access history has produced.

Transition: a single rotation

The rotation primitive is identical to the rotation in tutorials 26 and 27. It rewires three child pointers and three parent pointers, plus possibly updates the root pointer if the rotation happens at the top of the tree. After rotation, the BST invariant still holds because rotation preserves inorder, and the size invariant is restored locally by calling pull on the two affected nodes in the correct order: pull the node that descended first, then pull the node that rose. Routing every rotation through a single link(p, c, dir) helper that calls pull automatically eliminates a whole category of bugs.

Transition: one splay step

A splay step is one of zig, zig-zig, or zig-zag. Zig is one rotation; zig-zig and zig-zag are two rotations each. Each splay step strictly decreases the depth of x — by one if it was zig, by two otherwise — so the splay loop terminates after at most O(h) steps where h is the depth of x at the moment splay was called.

Termination

The splay loop's stopping condition is x is the root, which happens once the depth of x drops to zero. Because every splay step reduces the depth of x by at least one, the loop terminates after a finite number of iterations. The total number of rotations performed during a single splay is at most the original depth of x, and after termination, every node on the original access path has been re-arranged in the way the three local cases prescribe.

📜 08 · Proof of Correctness and the Potential Method

Correctness of splay-tree operations follows from two facts we already proved or assumed in tutorial 26: every rotation preserves the inorder of the subtree it acts on, and a BST's inorder is the sorted order of its keys. The splay loop is a sequence of rotations, so it preserves inorder and therefore the BST invariant. Insert, erase, find, kth, and order_of_key are all standard BST operations followed by a splay; the BST part places or finds the node correctly, and the splay does not break that correctness because it does not change the inorder. The interesting question is not whether splay trees are correct — they obviously are — but whether they are efficient. That is what the rest of this section addresses.

Why one operation is not enough to look at

A single splay can be expensive. Consider a tree that has degenerated into a left-leaning chain of n nodes, and suppose we splay the deepest leaf. The leaf's depth is n − 1, so the splay loop performs roughly n − 1 rotations and the operation costs Θ(n). There is no way to argue this single operation is cheap; it really is linear. The honest claim about splay trees is not "every operation costs O(log n)" but rather "any sequence of m operations on n nodes costs O((n + m) log n) total". To prove that, we need a tool that lets us aggregate the cost over a long sequence of operations and average across them. That tool is the potential method.

Step 1 — Telescoping sums, the prerequisite

Before we can use the potential method, we need one tiny piece of algebra: the telescoping sum. The idea is that if you take a sum of differences of consecutive terms, almost everything cancels and only the endpoints survive. Concretely, suppose we have a sequence of numbers Φ_0, Φ_1, Φ_2, ..., Φ_m. Look at the sum of consecutive differences:

(Φ_1 - Φ_0) + (Φ_2 - Φ_1) + (Φ_3 - Φ_2) + ... + (Φ_m - Φ_{m-1})

Now group the terms differently. The +Φ_1 inside the first parenthesis cancels with the -Φ_1 inside the second parenthesis. The +Φ_2 inside the second parenthesis cancels with the -Φ_2 inside the third. This cancellation continues all the way down, and only two terms survive: the -Φ_0 from the first parenthesis and the +Φ_m from the last one. The whole sum equals Φ_m − Φ_0. That is the entire content of a telescoping sum: a sum of consecutive differences collapses to "final minus initial", and everything in between disappears.

Step 2 — The potential method recipe

Pick any non-negative real-valued function Φ defined on the states of the data structure. Φ is called the potential. For our splay tree, Φ will eventually be a sum over all the nodes of a quantity called the node's rank, but for the moment treat Φ as a black box that maps "current tree" to a non-negative number.

Now define the amortized cost of an operation as follows. If the operation has actual cost c_i (measured in rotations, or any other concrete unit) and changes the potential from Φ_{i-1} before the operation to Φ_i after, then the amortized cost is defined to be

a_i = c_i + Φ_i - Φ_{i-1}.

Read this as: the amortized cost is the actual cost plus the change in potential. If an operation makes the tree "worse" (potential goes up), its amortized cost is larger than its actual cost; if it makes the tree "better" (potential goes down), its amortized cost is smaller than its actual cost, possibly even negative. The amortized cost is an accounting device, not a physical measurement.

Step 3 — Summing the amortized costs and letting the sum telescope

Sum the amortized costs over a sequence of m operations:

Σ_{i=1..m} a_i = Σ_{i=1..m} (c_i + Φ_i - Φ_{i-1}) = Σ_{i=1..m} c_i + Σ_{i=1..m} (Φ_i - Φ_{i-1}) = Σ_{i=1..m} c_i + (Φ_m - Φ_0) (the inner sum telescopes!)

Rearrange:

Σ_{i=1..m} c_i = Σ_{i=1..m} a_i + Φ_0 - Φ_m.

If the initial potential Φ_0 is zero and the final potential Φ_m is non-negative — and we chose Φ to always be non-negative, so Φ_m ≥ 0 automatically — then Φ_0 − Φ_m ≤ 0, and we get the inequality

Σ_{i=1..m} c_i ≤ Σ_{i=1..m} a_i.

Read this aloud: the total actual cost over the whole sequence is at most the total amortized cost. So if we can prove that every amortized cost is at most O(log n), then the total cost is at most m · O(log n) = O(m log n), and we have our amortized bound for free. The telescoping is what made it work: the bookkeeping terms Φ all cancelled in pairs, leaving only the endpoints.

Step 4 — The right potential function for splay trees

We now choose Φ. For each node x in the tree, define

s(x) = size of the subtree rooted at x (counting frq for multisets) r(x) = floor or real-valued log_2 (s(x)) (call r(x) the "rank" of x)

The rank is a logarithm of the subtree size. A node whose subtree is large has high rank; a leaf has rank 0. Define the potential of the whole tree as the sum of ranks:

Φ(tree) = Σ_{x in tree} r(x).

This Φ is non-negative because every r(x) is non-negative (subtree sizes are at least 1, so logarithms are at least 0). And the initial tree (with zero nodes) has Φ = 0. So the prerequisites for the telescoping argument hold: Φ_0 = 0 and Φ_m ≥ 0.

Step 5 — The Access Lemma

The central claim about splay trees is the Access Lemma, which bounds the amortized cost of a single splay operation by 3 times the change in the rank of the splayed node, plus a constant:

Access Lemma. Let x be the node being splayed, with rank r(x) before the splay, and let r(root) be the rank of the root before the splay. Then amortized cost of splay(x) ≤ 3 · (r(root) - r(x)) + 1.

Since r(root) ≤ log_2(n) (the root's subtree size is at most n) and r(x) ≥ 0, the right-hand side is at most 3 log_2(n) + 1, which is O(log n). Combined with the telescoping argument from step 3, this gives the amortized bound

Σ_{i=1..m} c_i ≤ Σ_{i=1..m} a_i ≤ Σ_{i=1..m} O(log n) = O(m log n).

Step 6 — Why each splay step pays for itself

The Access Lemma is proved one splay step at a time. For each step (zig, zig-zig, or zig-zag), we measure the actual cost (one rotation for zig, two rotations for the others) and the change in Φ caused by the step, and we check that the amortized cost is bounded by 3 · (r'(x) − r(x)) + (small correction), where r'(x) is the rank of x after the step and r(x) is its rank before. Summing over all the splay steps gives a telescoping sum on the rank of x: each step's r(x) after cancels with the next step's r(x) before, and only the very first rank r(x) and the very last rank r(root) survive. The constant +1 correction appears only on the final zig step, contributing a single +1 to the total.

The reason zig-zig is "the magic step" is precisely this per-step argument. A naive analysis of zig-zig that did not exploit its specific rotation order would charge two rotations to the amortized cost without any compensating drop in Φ, and the argument would fail. The zig-zig pattern's careful re-arrangement reduces the rank of the parent and grandparent by exactly enough to absorb the cost of the second rotation. Two consecutive zigs do not have this property; they would move x up but leave the path unfolded, and the amortized analysis would not go through. The Access Lemma is the precise technical reason the textbook insists on the zig-zig ordering.

Putting it all together

The splay tree's amortized O(log n) per operation is the conclusion of a four-step chain: (1) every splay is a sequence of zig, zig-zig, and zig-zag steps; (2) each step's amortized cost telescopes against the rank of x, leaving only the first and last terms; (3) the surviving terms sum to at most 3 r(root) + 1 = O(log n) by the Access Lemma; (4) the total actual cost over m operations is bounded by the total amortized cost, which is at most m · O(log n) = O(m log n) by the telescoping identity in step 3. Each link in the chain is tight; weakening any of them breaks the bound.

⏱️ 09 · Complexity Analysis

The Access Lemma gives us amortized O(log n) per public operation. Insert, erase, find, count, kth, and order_of_key each consist of a constant number of BST descents plus one or two splays, and each descent walks the same path the splay will then climb. The descent cost is paid for by the splay's amortized bound; the descent and the splay together still cost amortized O(log n).

The working-set bound

Beyond the basic amortized O(log n), splay trees enjoy a stronger guarantee called the working-set bound. If at the time of an access the target node has been one of the last k distinct nodes accessed, the amortized cost of that access is O(log k) rather than O(log n). This means that if your workload has a hot working set of size k, splay trees automatically run as fast as if you had a balanced tree of size k, regardless of how many other elements the structure contains. No other balanced BST gives this for free.

The static-optimality bound

A second remarkable property: on any fixed sequence of access requests, splay trees are within a constant factor of the cost achieved by the best possible static BST for that sequence — the BST you would build if you knew the access frequencies in advance and chose the tree shape to minimise expected access cost. So splay trees are, up to constants, never worse than the best offline static structure, even though they have no advance knowledge of the access distribution. Splay trees achieve this without storing any frequencies or hint information; the self-adjustment is the entire mechanism.

The dynamic-optimality conjecture

There is a famous open conjecture, due to Sleator and Tarjan, that splay trees are within a constant factor of the cost achieved by the best possible dynamic BST algorithm for any sequence — including algorithms that may rotate arbitrarily between accesses based on the access history. This is the Dynamic Optimality Conjecture, and it has been open since 1985. It is not needed for any of the bounds we use in practice; it is mentioned here because it explains why splay trees remain an active topic of theoretical research decades after their invention.

🧱 10 · Template Philosophy

Both splay templates in this tutorial share four design choices that match the rest of the Coach Academy treap family.

Choice 1: rotate(child), not rotate(parent, direction)

The rotation primitive takes the child that should rise, recovers its parent from the child's parent pointer, and recovers the direction from which slot of the parent the child occupies. This is the same convention as the key treap of tutorial 27, and it eliminates the bug class where the caller passes the wrong direction. The rotate function itself must remember to save the grandparent direction pdir = getDir(g, p) before any rewiring happens, because once the rotation runs, p is no longer a child of g and that direction can no longer be recovered.

Choice 2: a sentinel node makes missing children safe to read

The key-splay multiset version uses an EMPTY sentinel, and the implicit-splay sequence version uses the same idea with a sentinel named E. In both templates, the sentinel has size zero (and frequency zero where frq exists), its children point at itself, and its parent points at itself. As a result, reads like u->ch[L]->sz are always safe and return zero on missing children, and the size formula composes correctly without null checks. Template B additionally stores parent pointers and a lazy rev flag on real nodes; the sentinel E acts as the zero-size missing child everywhere a child slot might be empty.

Choice 3: link(p, c, dir) is the only mutator of child pointers

Every place that would write p->ch[dir] = c goes through link instead. The helper sets the child slot, sets c's parent to p if c is not the sentinel, and calls pull(p) to refresh the subtree size. Routing all writes through one helper means subtree sizes and parent pointers stay correct by construction; the rotation function becomes three link calls plus a root-update branch.

Choice 4: splay-after-every-touch is non-negotiable

Every public method ends by calling splay on a node it has just inspected. If you write a new method that does a BST descent, end it with a splay; otherwise the amortized bound silently breaks. The line-by-line walkthrough in section 12 calls out the splay site of each method.

🎯 11 · Master Templates — Ready to Submit

Two production templates are provided below, each in the same Coach Academy style used by tutorials 27, 28, and 29. Template A is a true key splay multiset with frq, the splay analogue of the key treap from tutorial 27. Template B is a true implicit splay sequence with split and merge, the splay analogue of the implicit treap from tutorial 28. Pick whichever matches your problem; the two are separate in code.

Template A — Key Splay Multiset with frq

Around 250 lines. Supports insert, eraseOne, contains, count, kth, order_of_key, first_ge, last_le, split_less, join, and a debug pretty-printer. The kth indexing convention is 0-based: kth(0) returns the smallest occurrence, kth(size − 1) the largest.

// Key Splay Tree Multiset with frq — rotate(child) style
#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;
enum { L = 0, R = 1 };

struct Node {
    long long v;
    int sz, frq;
    Node *p, *ch[2];

    Node() : v(0), sz(0), frq(0), p(this), ch{this, this} {}
    Node(long long _v, Node* EMPTY)
        : v(_v), sz(1), frq(1), p(EMPTY), ch{EMPTY, EMPTY} {}

    void push() {} // no lazy in the key splay; kept for symmetry with implicit
    void pull() { sz = ch[L]->sz + frq + ch[R]->sz; }
};

Node* EMPTY = new Node();

int getDir(Node* p, Node* c) { return p->ch[R] == c ? R : L; }

void link(Node* p, Node* c, int dir) {
    if (c != EMPTY) c->p = p;
    if (p != EMPTY) { p->ch[dir] = c; p->pull(); }
}

struct SplayTree {
    Node* root;
    SplayTree() : root(EMPTY) {}

    int size() const { return root->sz; }

    void setRoot(Node* u) {
        root = u;
        if (root != EMPTY) root->p = EMPTY;
    }

    // rotate child c above its parent; save grandparent direction first.
    void rotate(Node* c) {
        Node* p = c->p;
        Node* g = p->p;
        int d = getDir(p, c);
        int gd = (g == EMPTY ? L : getDir(g, p)); // save BEFORE any rewiring
        p->push(); c->push();
        Node* middle = c->ch[d ^ 1];
        link(p, middle, d);
        link(c, p, d ^ 1);
        if (g == EMPTY) setRoot(c);
        else link(g, c, gd);
    }

    // splay x up to the goal (default: root).
    void splay(Node* x, Node* goal = nullptr) {
        if (x == EMPTY) return;
        if (goal == nullptr) goal = EMPTY;
        while (x->p != goal) {
            Node* p = x->p;
            Node* g = p->p;
            if (g == goal) rotate(x);                   // zig
            else if (getDir(p, x) == getDir(g, p)) { // zig-zig: rotate p FIRST
                rotate(p); rotate(x);
            } else {                                 // zig-zag: rotate x twice
                rotate(x); rotate(x);
            }
        }
        if (goal == EMPTY) setRoot(x);
    }

    Node* findNode(long long x, bool splayLast = true) {
        Node *u = root, *last = EMPTY;
        while (u != EMPTY) {
            last = u;
            if (x == u->v) { splay(u); return u; }
            u = u->ch[u->v < x];
        }
        if (splayLast && last != EMPTY) splay(last);
        return EMPTY;
    }

    bool contains(long long x) { return findNode(x) != EMPTY; }

    int count(long long x) {
        Node* u = findNode(x);
        return (u == EMPTY ? 0 : u->frq);
    }

    void insert(long long x) {
        if (root == EMPTY) { setRoot(new Node(x, EMPTY)); return; }
        Node *u = root, *par = EMPTY;
        while (u != EMPTY) {
            par = u;
            if (x == u->v) { ++u->frq; u->pull(); splay(u); return; }
            u = u->ch[u->v < x];
        }
        Node* nw = new Node(x, EMPTY);
        link(par, nw, par->v < x);
        splay(nw);
    }

    Node* maximum(Node* u) const { while (u->ch[R] != EMPTY) u = u->ch[R]; return u; }
    Node* minimum(Node* u) const { while (u->ch[L] != EMPTY) u = u->ch[L]; return u; }

    // Join two splay trees a and b where every key in a is strictly less than every key in b.
    Node* join(Node* a, Node* b) {
        if (a == EMPTY) { if (b != EMPTY) b->p = EMPTY; return b; }
        if (b == EMPTY) { a->p = EMPTY; return a; }
        setRoot(a);
        splay(maximum(root)); // after this, root has no right child
        link(root, b, R);
        return root;
    }

    void eraseOne(long long x) {
        Node* u = findNode(x);
        if (u == EMPTY) return;
        if (u->frq > 1) { --u->frq; u->pull(); return; }
        // u is at root (findNode splayed it); detach its two children and join them.
        Node *leftTree = u->ch[L], *rightTree = u->ch[R];
        if (leftTree != EMPTY) leftTree->p = EMPTY;
        if (rightTree != EMPTY) rightTree->p = EMPTY;
        u->ch[L] = u->ch[R] = EMPTY;
        delete u;
        setRoot(join(leftTree, rightTree));
    }

    Node* kthNode(int k) {
        if (k < 0 || k >= size()) return EMPTY;
        Node* u = root;
        while (u != EMPTY) {
            u->push();
            int leftSize = u->ch[L]->sz;
            if (k < leftSize) u = u->ch[L];
            else if (k < leftSize + u->frq) { splay(u); return u; }
            else { k -= leftSize + u->frq; u = u->ch[R]; }
        }
        return EMPTY;
    }

    long long kth(int k) {
        Node* u = kthNode(k);
        return (u == EMPTY ? LLONG_MIN : u->v);
    }

    int order_of_key(long long x) {
        Node *u = root, *last = EMPTY;
        int ans = 0;
        while (u != EMPTY) {
            last = u;
            if (x <= u->v) u = u->ch[L];
            else { ans += u->ch[L]->sz + u->frq; u = u->ch[R]; }
        }
        // Splay the last touched node to preserve the splay-after-every-touch discipline.
        if (last != EMPTY) splay(last);
        return ans;
    }

    long long first_ge(long long x) {
        Node *u = root, *ans = EMPTY, *last = EMPTY;
        while (u != EMPTY) {
            last = u;
            if (u->v >= x) { ans = u; u = u->ch[L]; }
            else u = u->ch[R];
        }
        // Splay the candidate if one was found, otherwise splay the last touched node
        // so failed boundary searches still obey the splay-after-every-touch rule.
        Node* touch = (ans != EMPTY ? ans : last);
        if (touch != EMPTY) splay(touch);
        return (ans == EMPTY ? LLONG_MAX : ans->v);
    }

    long long last_le(long long x) {
        Node *u = root, *ans = EMPTY, *last = EMPTY;
        while (u != EMPTY) {
            last = u;
            if (u->v <= x) { ans = u; u = u->ch[R]; }
            else u = u->ch[L];
        }
        // Splay the candidate if one was found, otherwise splay the last touched node
        // so failed boundary searches still obey the splay-after-every-touch rule.
        Node* touch = (ans != EMPTY ? ans : last);
        if (touch != EMPTY) splay(touch);
        return (ans == EMPTY ? LLONG_MIN : ans->v);
    }

    // Split into (values < x) and (values >= x). The current tree is consumed.
    pair<Node*, Node*> split_less(long long x) {
        if (root == EMPTY) return {EMPTY, EMPTY};
        long long ge = first_ge(x);
        if (ge == LLONG_MAX) {
            Node* left = root; setRoot(EMPTY);
            return {left, EMPTY};
        }
        // after first_ge, the smallest value >= x is at the root.
        Node* right = root;
        Node* left = right->ch[L];
        if (left != EMPTY) left->p = EMPTY;
        link(right, EMPTY, L);
        setRoot(EMPTY);
        return {left, right};
    }

    void inorder(Node* u, vector<long long>& a) const {
        if (u == EMPTY) return;
        inorder(u->ch[L], a);
        for (int i = 0; i < u->frq; ++i) a.push_back(u->v);
        inorder(u->ch[R], a);
    }
    vector<long long> to_vector() const {
        vector<long long> a; inorder(root, a); return a;
    }
};

Template B — Implicit Splay Tree with Split / Merge and Lazy Reverse

Around 280 lines. The same algorithmic chassis as Template A, but with three differences: no frq field (each sequence position is its own node), no value-keyed BST descent (positions are derived from subtree sizes), and a lazy rev flag for range reverse. The public API is built around split and merge primitives implemented in terms of splay, matching the implicit-treap convention from tutorial 28.

// Implicit Splay Tree: sequence with split/merge/reverse via splay
#include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;
enum { L = 0, R = 1 };

struct Node {
    long long v, sum;
    int sz;
    bool rev;
    Node *p, *ch[2];
    Node() : v(0), sum(0), sz(0), rev(false), p(this), ch{this, this} {}
    Node(long long _v, Node* E)
        : v(_v), sum(_v), sz(1), rev(false), p(E), ch{E, E} {}

    void applyRev() {
        if (sz == 0) return;
        swap(ch[L], ch[R]);
        rev ^= 1;
    }
    void push() {
        if (!rev) return;
        ch[L]->applyRev();
        ch[R]->applyRev();
        rev = false;
    }
    void pull() {
        sz = ch[L]->sz + 1 + ch[R]->sz;
        sum = ch[L]->sum + v + ch[R]->sum;
    }
};

Node* E = new Node();

int getDir(Node* p, Node* c) { return p->ch[R] == c ? R : L; }

void link(Node* p, Node* c, int dir) {
    if (c != E) c->p = p;
    if (p != E) { p->ch[dir] = c; p->pull(); }
}

struct ImplicitSplay {
    Node* root;
    ImplicitSplay() : root(E) {}

    int size() const { return root->sz; }

    void setRoot(Node* u) {
        root = u;
        if (root != E) root->p = E;
    }

    void rotate(Node* c) {
        Node* p = c->p;
        Node* g = p->p;
        int d = getDir(p, c);
        int gd = (g == E ? L : getDir(g, p));
        p->push(); c->push();
        Node* middle = c->ch[d ^ 1];
        link(p, middle, d);
        link(c, p, d ^ 1);
        if (g == E) setRoot(c);
        else link(g, c, gd);
    }

    // Push the lazy flag down from the root toward x before splaying x,
    // so descent decisions in rotate() are based on the post-push layout.
    void pushFromRoot(Node* x) {
        if (x == E) return;
        pushFromRoot(x->p);
        x->push();
    }

    void splay(Node* x, Node* goal = nullptr) {
        if (x == E) return;
        if (goal == nullptr) goal = E;
        pushFromRoot(x);
        while (x->p != goal) {
            Node* p = x->p;
            Node* g = p->p;
            if (g == goal) rotate(x);
            else if (getDir(p, x) == getDir(g, p)) { rotate(p); rotate(x); }
            else { rotate(x); rotate(x); }
        }
        if (goal == E) setRoot(x);
    }

    // Splay the node at position k (0-indexed) to the root.
    Node* kthNode(int k) {
        if (k < 0 || k >= size()) return E;
        Node* u = root;
        while (u != E) {
            u->push();
            int leftSize = u->ch[L]->sz;
            if (k < leftSize) u = u->ch[L];
            else if (k == leftSize) { splay(u); return u; }
            else { k -= leftSize + 1; u = u->ch[R]; }
        }
        return E;
    }

    // Merge two implicit-splay trees: every position in a comes before every position in b.
    Node* merge(Node* a, Node* b) {
        if (a == E) { if (b != E) b->p = E; return b; }
        if (b == E) { a->p = E; return a; }
        // Splay a's rightmost node to the root, then attach b as its right subtree.
        setRoot(a);
        Node* mx = a;
        mx->push();
        while (mx->ch[R] != E) { mx = mx->ch[R]; mx->push(); }
        splay(mx);
        link(root, b, R);
        return root;
    }

    // Split into (first leftSize elements, remaining elements). The input is consumed.
    pair<Node*, Node*> split(Node* u, int leftSize) {
        if (u == E) return {E, E};
        setRoot(u);
        if (leftSize == 0) {
            Node* right = root; right->p = E; root = E;
            return {E, right};
        }
        if (leftSize >= root->sz) {
            Node* left = root; left->p = E; root = E;
            return {left, E};
        }
        // Splay the (leftSize-1)-th node, detach its right subtree.
        Node* x = kthNode(leftSize - 1);
        Node* right = x->ch[R];
        if (right != E) right->p = E;
        link(x, E, R);
        Node* left = x; left->p = E;
        return {left, right};
    }

    void build(const vector<long long>& a) {
        root = E;
        for (long long x : a) root = merge(root, new Node(x, E));
    }

    void insert(int pos, long long x) {
        auto [a, b] = split(root, pos);
        root = merge(merge(a, new Node(x, E)), b);
        setRoot(root);
    }

    void erase(int pos) {
        auto [a, b] = split(root, pos);
        auto [mid, c] = split(b, 1);
        if (mid != E) delete mid;
        root = merge(a, c);
        setRoot(root);
    }

    void reverseRange(int l, int r) {
        if (l > r) return;
        auto [a, b] = split(root, r + 1);
        auto [c, d] = split(a, l);
        d->applyRev();
        root = merge(merge(c, d), b);
        setRoot(root);
    }

    long long rangeSum(int l, int r) {
        if (l > r) return 0;
        auto [a, b] = split(root, r + 1);
        auto [c, d] = split(a, l);
        long long ans = d->sum;
        root = merge(merge(c, d), b);
        setRoot(root);
        return ans;
    }

    long long get(int pos) {
        Node* u = kthNode(pos);
        return (u == E ? 0 : u->v);
    }

    void inorder(Node* u, vector<long long>& a) {
        if (u == E) return;
        u->push();
        inorder(u->ch[L], a);
        a.push_back(u->v);
        inorder(u->ch[R], a);
    }
    vector<long long> to_vector() {
        vector<long long> a; inorder(root, a); return a;
    }
};

📝 12 · Line-by-Line Walkthrough

The two templates share a common chassis. We walk through Template A in detail; the differences in Template B (no frq, lazy reverse, push-from-root before splay) are described where they diverge.

Node struct, EMPTY sentinel, helper functions

The Node struct carries six fields: the key v, the subtree size sz, the frequency frq for multiset duplicates, the parent pointer p, and the two child pointers in ch[2]. The default constructor produces the EMPTY sentinel, whose parent and children point at itself and whose sz, frq are zero. Reads like u->ch[L]->sz always return zero on a missing child, so the size formula composes correctly without null checks. The getDir function recovers whether a child sits in the left or right slot of its parent, and link performs the only child-pointer write in the entire class: it sets the slot, sets the child's parent, and calls pull.

rotate(c) with the saved gd

The rotate function takes the child that should rise. It reads p = c->p and g = p->p, computes d = getDir(p, c) for the direction of c under p, and then — critically — saves gd = getDir(g, p) before any rewiring happens. The save is essential because the second link call below moves p to become a child of c, so after that point p is no longer a child of g and reading the direction would return the wrong slot. The three rewiring calls perform the standard rotation: c's opposite-side child moves to become p's same-side child, p becomes c's opposite-side child, and c takes p's old slot under g using the saved gd. If there was no grandparent, c becomes the new root via setRoot.

splay(x, goal) and the three cases

The splay function lifts x until its parent equals the goal (or the sentinel EMPTY if no goal was specified, meaning lift all the way to the root). Inside the loop, the parent and grandparent are inspected to choose one of the three cases. Zig fires when the grandparent equals the goal — only one rotation away from termination. Zig-zig fires when x and its parent are on the same side; the rotation order is parent first, then x, and skipping this order silently breaks the amortized bound (see pitfall 4 of section 14). Zig-zag fires when they are on opposite sides; the order is x twice. The implicit-splay template adds one extra step at the top: pushFromRoot(x) walks up from x to the root and pushes lazy flags down on every ancestor, ensuring that subsequent rotations work on the post-push layout rather than a stale one.

findNode, insert, eraseOne

The find descent walks down the BST. If it finds a matching node, it splays the matching node to the root and returns it. If it walks off the tree, it splays the last node it touched, then returns EMPTY. Splaying the last touched node is the trick that activates the amortized bound on unsuccessful searches; without it, repeated failed lookups for nearby missing values would accumulate cost without rebalancing. Insert either bumps frq on an existing node and splays it, or attaches a fresh leaf via link and splays the new leaf. EraseOne splays the doomed node to the root, then either decrements frq (if it was greater than one) or removes the root entirely and joins the two surviving subtrees through the join primitive.

join(a, b)

The join primitive assumes every key in a is strictly less than every key in b. The strategy: temporarily make a the root, splay the maximum of a up to the top — which leaves a's new root with no right child by virtue of being the maximum — and then link b as the right child of that new root. The cost of finding the maximum is bounded by the amortized cost of the splay it triggers, so the whole join is amortized O(log n).

kth, order_of_key, first_ge, last_le, split_less

kth descends from the root, using subtree sizes to decide whether the desired rank lies in the left subtree, at the current node (matching the run of frq occurrences), or in the right subtree. Once found, the matching node is splayed to the root. order_of_key descends accumulating the count of strictly-less-than occurrences by adding sz(left) + frq every time the descent moves right; it remembers the last non-EMPTY node it touched, and after the rank is computed it splays that node to the root to preserve the splay-after-every-touch discipline (which is why order_of_key is not const). The rank is captured before splaying so the answer is unaffected by the rotations that follow. first_ge and last_le remember the most recent candidate that satisfies the inequality; if such a candidate exists, they splay it, otherwise they splay the last touched node so failed boundary searches still obey the splay-after-every-touch rule. split_less locates the smallest value not less than x, splays it to the root, detaches its left subtree, and returns the two halves; if no such value exists, the entire tree goes to the left half and the right half is empty.

What Template B changes

Template B drops frq (each sequence position is one node), uses subtree sizes alone to derive position-keyed descent, and adds the lazy rev flag. The crucial difference for correctness is the pushFromRoot call at the top of splay: when a lazy reverse is pending on some ancestor of x, that ancestor's child pointers are swapped lazily, and splay's rotation cases would read the swapped layout incorrectly unless the lazy is pushed down first. The split and merge primitives are also reformulated in terms of splay rather than the recursive-descent style of the implicit treap: split splays the boundary element to the root and detaches its right subtree, merge splays a's maximum to the top and links b as its right child.

🧩 13 · Edge Cases (Centralized Audit)

A consolidated checklist of boundary conditions every splay-tree implementation must handle. Most splay bugs trace back to violating one of these. The checks below are written for Template A unless noted; Template B has analogous concerns wherever the operation exists.

  • Empty tree. root equals EMPTY. Insert into empty creates the first node and sets it as root without calling splay (splay on a node that is already the root would be a no-op anyway). Find, kth, order_of_key all terminate immediately with their empty-case results.
  • Insert into empty. Special-cased at the top of insert. The new node becomes the root and the function returns; calling splay on the only node in the tree is unnecessary work.
  • Insert a duplicate. The descent finds the matching node, bumps its frq, calls pull on it, then splays it. The tree shape changes via the splay but no new node is allocated.
  • Erase a value that is absent. findNode walks off the tree and splays the last touched node. eraseOne returns immediately without modifying anything further. The splay-the-last-touched-node behavior is essential for the amortized bound on failed accesses.
  • Erase the root with two non-empty children. After splaying the doomed node to the root, its two children become exposed. detach both, join them, set the joined result as the new root.
  • Erase the root with only one child. The join primitive's empty-side branches handle this. Joining EMPTY with a non-empty tree returns the non-empty tree unchanged.
  • Erase the only node in the tree. Both children are EMPTY; join returns EMPTY; the tree becomes empty cleanly.
  • kth with k out of range. Explicit bounds check returns EMPTY at the top of kthNode; the wrapper kth returns LLONG_MIN as a sentinel.
  • order_of_key smaller than the minimum value. Returns 0 (no elements strictly less than x).
  • order_of_key larger than the maximum value. Returns size() (every element is strictly less than x).
  • first_ge / last_le with no candidate. Returns LLONG_MAX / LLONG_MIN respectively.
  • Splay called on EMPTY. Guarded at the top of the splay function; an EMPTY splay is a no-op.
  • setRoot called with EMPTY. Sets root to EMPTY without trying to write EMPTY's parent (which would mutate the sentinel).
  • Implicit-splay: range with l > r. Both reverseRange and rangeSum guard at the top and return early.
  • Implicit-splay: lazy flag interaction with subsequent splay. pushFromRoot must be called before any descent through a node with pending rev, or the rotation case decisions read the pre-push (incorrect) layout.

🪲 14 · Common Pitfalls and Bugs

Pitfall 1 — Forgetting splay after every touch
Every public method that descends through the tree must end with a splay on some node it visited. Skipping the splay does not cause a correctness bug — the BST invariant still holds — but it silently breaks the amortized bound and the tree may degrade into a chain over a long sequence of operations.
Pitfall 2 — Broken parent pointers
Every non-root node must point at its true parent, and every root at EMPTY. The rotation primitive and the splay loop both inspect parent pointers; a stale or wrong parent corrupts the tree immediately. Routing every child-pointer write through link is the cleanest defense.
Pitfall 3 — Reading gd after rewiring
In rotate, the direction of the parent under the grandparent must be saved before the second link call moves the parent under c. Reading getDir(g, p) after that point returns the wrong slot and attaches the rotated subtree to the wrong side of the grandparent — the exact bug that the corrected key-treap rotation in tutorial 27 addresses.
Pitfall 4 — Wrong zig-zig order
In zig-zig, the parent must be rotated first and then x; doing two consecutive rotates of x instead is the zig-zag pattern and produces a different final shape. Both shapes preserve the BST invariant, so the correctness tests pass, but the amortized bound fails: the access path is not folded the way the Access Lemma requires, and pathological access sequences can blow up the cost.
Pitfall 5 — Forgetting frq in the size formula
In Template A, the size formula must be sz = sz(left) + frq + sz(right). Writing the BST formula sz = sz(left) + 1 + sz(right) silently breaks kth and order_of_key on any input containing duplicates. The implicit variant does not have frq, so the correct formula there is the one-per-node form.
Pitfall 6 — Implicit splay: skipping pushFromRoot before splay
When a lazy reverse is pending on an ancestor of x, the ancestor's children are swapped lazily. Calling splay without first pushing the flag down from the root causes the rotation case decisions in the splay loop to read the pre-push (incorrect) layout, and the tree silently produces wrong query answers on subsequent operations.
Pitfall 7 — Mutating EMPTY
The sentinel's six fields are part of the invariant: v=0, sz=0, frq=0, parent and both children pointing at itself. If a careless write accidentally sets EMPTY->p or one of EMPTY->ch[i] to a real node, every future descent through that slot misbehaves. The link helper guards against this by checking if (c != EMPTY) c->p = p; and by checking if (p != EMPTY) before writing the child slot.

🧭 15 · Decision Guide

When the splay tree is the right tool

need self-adjustment to access patterns ⇒ splay (working-set bound)
need deterministic amortized O(log n) ⇒ splay (treap is expected; splay is deterministic)
need worst-case O(log n) per operation ⇒ AVL or Red-Black (splay is amortized only)
need ordered-set operations only ⇒ std::set or PBDS (shorter than rolling splay)
need a stepping stone to link-cut trees ⇒ splay (link-cut is built on splay)
need range reverse / split / merge on a sequence ⇒ implicit splay (Template B) or implicit treap (tutorial 28)
need historical versions ⇒ persistent treap or persistent segment tree, not this splay tutorial

🎭 16 · Variants and Extensions

Variant A — Link-Cut Trees

The link-cut tree is a dynamic-tree data structure that maintains a forest of rooted trees under link, cut, find-root, and path-query operations, all in amortized O(log n). Its core is a collection of splay trees, one per "preferred path" in the represented forest. Splaying inside a preferred path is the basic step, and the analysis re-uses the Access Lemma. Link-cut trees are the standard solution for problems like dynamic-tree connectivity, dynamic minimum spanning forest, and the offline maximum-flow blocking step. Learning the splay tree is a prerequisite; the link-cut tree adds the preferred-path machinery on top.

Variant B — Top Trees

A more general dynamic-tree structure that supports arbitrary path and subtree aggregates with O(log n) updates. Top trees use either splay trees or balanced biased trees as their internal representation. They are heavier than link-cut trees but expose a richer API; problems requiring subtree sums on dynamic trees often reach for top trees.

Variant C — Finger Trees and Fingered Splay

If consecutive accesses tend to be close to each other in the inorder, a "finger" pointer that remembers the most recent access can speed things up further. A fingered splay tree splays from the finger upward toward the new access, then back down, paying only O(log d) where d is the distance between the finger and the new access. The technique earns its place in workloads with strong sequential locality.

Variant D — Augmented Aggregates

Template B augments each node with a subtree sum; the technique generalises to any commutative-associative aggregate. Add a field to Node, update pull to combine the children's aggregates and the current node's value, and every range-query routine continues to work. For non-commutative aggregates, the applyRev routine must also flip the aggregate's internal ordering, mirroring the implicit-treap convention from tutorial 28.

Variant E — Top-down Splay

The splay implementation in this tutorial is bottom-up: we walk down the tree to find x, then splay x upward case by case. An alternative is the top-down splay, which performs rotations on the descent itself and never needs to walk back up. Top-down splay has slightly better cache behavior on some architectures and avoids the recursive stack; it is functionally equivalent. The textbook treatment in Sleator and Tarjan's original paper presents both versions.

Variant F — Working-Set Specialised Splay

If you know in advance that your workload has a small hot working set, you can lean further into the working-set behavior by splaying only sometimes (every k-th access) or by using deeper-cost-amortisation tricks. These specialised variants are mostly of theoretical interest; the plain splay tree already captures most of the benefit.

🎯 18 · Final Summary

One sentence: a splay tree is a BST with one rule — splay every accessed node to the root — and the rule alone, executed through the zig / zig-zig / zig-zag pattern, gives amortized O(log n) per operation by way of the potential method and the Access Lemma.

Five things to remember

First, splay trees keep only the BST invariant, the size invariant, and the parent invariant — no balance condition, no color, no priority. Second, the splay loop applies one of three local cases (zig, zig-zig, zig-zag) depending on the relative positions of the splayed node, its parent, and its grandparent, and zig-zig is the case whose specific rotation order makes the amortized analysis work. Third, the amortized bound comes from the potential method: pick the potential Φ to be the sum of log-subtree-sizes ranks, sum the amortized costs over a sequence of operations, let the inner sum telescope, and the surviving terms are bounded by O(log n) per operation. Fourth, every public method must end with a splay or the amortized bound silently breaks. Fifth, the splay tree is the foundation for link-cut trees and top trees, which is why every serious data-structures education includes it even where treaps would otherwise suffice.

Mastery checklist

  • I can write the rotate function with the saved gd from memory in under five minutes.
  • I can name the three splay cases and draw the before-and-after diagrams of each by hand.
  • I can write the splay loop without referring to the master template.
  • I can recite the potential method recipe: define Φ, define amortized cost, sum and telescope.
  • I can state the Access Lemma and explain why it implies amortized O(log n) per operation.
  • I can extend Template B with a new aggregate (max, gcd, hash) by adding one field and updating pull.
  • I know when to reach for splay over a treap (deterministic, working-set, stepping stone to link-cut).
